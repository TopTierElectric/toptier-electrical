Solid-strand aluminum branch wiring was used in many U.S. homes built between roughly 1965 and 1972. It works — until it doesn't. Aluminum expands and contracts more than copper under load, and aging connections can loosen, oxidize, and overheat. The Consumer Product Safety Commission and CPSC studies have linked it to elevated fire risk.

If you have it, you have real options. None of them require panic; all of them require honesty about the risk.

## Quick Answer

If your home has aluminum branch wiring:

- **Don't ignore it.** Loose, hot, or oxidized aluminum connections are a real fire risk.
- **Don't rip it all out unless necessary.** Full rewires are expensive and disruptive.
- **The standard remediation is pigtailing or COPALUM crimping** — connect a short copper tail at every device using a method rated for aluminum.

Get an electrician who has worked on aluminum to assess and recommend an approach.

## What Aluminum Branch Wiring Is (And Isn't)

Two different things often get confused:

1. **Aluminum service entrance conductors** — the large wires from the meter to the panel. These are aluminum in many homes (old and new) and are not the safety concern. Aluminum is the standard material for service-size conductors.

2. **Aluminum branch circuit wiring** — the smaller wires that run from the panel to outlets, switches, and lights. Solid-strand aluminum branch wiring (mostly 1965–1972) is the safety concern this post is about.

If you're not sure which you have, an electrician can tell in five minutes by pulling a panel cover and looking at a few receptacles.

## How to Tell If You Have Aluminum Branch Wiring

- **Home age.** Built between 1965 and 1972 is the highest-probability era. Some 1960s and early-1970s homes used it; few before, almost none after.
- **Stamped wire markings.** Cable jackets are stamped "AL" or "ALUMINUM" — visible at receptacles and switches when the cover plate is removed.
- **Wire color (with cable jacket removed).** Aluminum is silver/gray; copper is, well, copper-colored. (This requires opening a device — leave that to the electrician.)
- **Panel inspection.** The electrician can confirm by looking at conductors landed on breaker terminals.

Realistic check: pull a few receptacles in different rooms and inspect. Don't rely only on the panel — sometimes mixed wiring exists.

## The Real Risk

The risk isn't the aluminum itself — it's the connections. At every receptacle, switch, splice, and breaker termination, the aluminum wire mechanically contacts another conductor (or screw, or wire nut). Three things can go wrong:

1. **Thermal cycling.** Aluminum expands and contracts more than copper. Over thousands of heating cycles (current flowing, not flowing), the connection loosens.
2. **Oxidation.** Aluminum oxide is non-conductive. Where copper develops a thin oxide layer that conducts well enough, aluminum's oxide layer increases resistance.
3. **Galvanic corrosion.** Aluminum and copper in contact with moisture create a small battery effect that corrodes the connection.

Loose + oxidized + corroded = high resistance = heat. Heat burns insulation. Burning insulation starts fires.

## Your Three Options

### 1. Pigtailing With Listed Connectors

The standard approach: at every receptacle, switch, and junction box, the aluminum wire is connected to a short copper "pigtail" using a connector specifically listed for aluminum-to-copper splices (usually purple wire nuts rated for AL/CU, or specialized lever connectors).

The copper pigtail then connects to the receptacle or switch. The aluminum-to-copper junction is engineered to handle thermal cycling and corrosion safely.

Pros: faster, cheaper, less disruptive than rewiring.
Cons: every device must be done correctly; partial work doesn't help.

### 2. COPALUM Crimps

A higher-grade approach using specialized crimped connectors that mechanically lock aluminum to copper. CPSC has historically endorsed this as the gold-standard remediation.

Pros: arguably the most reliable long-term remediation.
Cons: requires specialized tooling; not every electrician offers it; per-device cost is higher.

### 3. Full Rewire

Replace the aluminum branch wiring with copper. The "no aluminum, no problem" option.

Pros: removes the failure mode entirely.
Cons: most expensive, most disruptive (drywall work, finish repair, often staged over weeks).

When a full rewire makes sense:

- The home is already being remodeled (walls open anyway)
- The wiring has additional issues beyond aluminum (knob-and-tube remnants, ungrounded circuits, undersized for current code)
- The buyer of the home demands it as a sale condition

## What an Electrician Checks During an Aluminum Assessment

- **Every receptacle and switch** — looking for discoloration, scorch marks, melted insulation, or loose terminations
- **Every panel termination** — aluminum landed on breakers compatible with aluminum (CO/ALR or AL/CU rated)
- **Junction boxes** — splices made with appropriate connectors
- **Specific high-risk devices** — high-current loads (kitchen, dryer, AC) where heat is more likely

If the assessment finds devices already showing damage, those circuits get prioritized.

## Cost and Scope Factors

Aluminum remediation pricing depends on:

- Number of devices in the home (receptacles, switches, junctions)
- Whether COPALUM or listed connectors
- Accessibility (slab construction can complicate rerouting)
- Panel work (sometimes panel termination upgrades are part of scope)
- Whether bundled with other code corrections

Most pigtail-remediations on a typical home complete in a few days; COPALUM jobs take longer due to the specialized crimping process.

## What to Do Right Now (If You Suspect Aluminum)

1. **Don't run high loads on suspicious circuits.** If a receptacle is warm to the touch, scorched, or buzzing — stop using it. Call.
2. **Schedule an assessment.** This is a quick visit; the electrician confirms what you have and outlines options.
3. **Tell your insurance company.** Some insurers ask about aluminum during home inspection or annual review. Honest disclosure usually doesn't change anything as long as remediation is in progress or complete.
4. **Document.** Save the assessment report, scope, and after-photos. Useful for resale and for insurance.

## FAQs

**My home is from 1965–1972 — does that mean I have aluminum wiring?**
Maybe. Some homes from that era used copper. The only way to know is to look. An electrician can confirm in a single visit.

**Are "purple wire nuts" the same as proper aluminum-to-copper connectors?**
Not all purple wire nuts are AL/CU rated. The connector must be specifically listed for aluminum splicing — read the packaging.

**Can I just replace receptacles with CO/ALR-rated devices?**
This was historically allowed but is no longer considered an adequate full remediation by CPSC. Pigtailing or COPALUM is the current standard.

**Will pigtailing affect my home insurance?**
Usually positively — completed remediation often resolves insurer concerns. Check with your specific carrier.
