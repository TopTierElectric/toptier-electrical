EV charger placement looks like a simple decision until you've lived with one for a year. The wrong location means dragging a 30-pound cable across the garage every night, scraping it against a wall, or fishing it under a hatchback every morning. The right location makes plugging in as automatic as parking. Here is how to think about garage layout before the electrician shows up.

## Quick Answer

- Mount the charger **on the wall closest to your EV's charge port**.
- Standard mount height is **48 inches** from the floor.
- Use a **20–25 foot cable** for flexibility — not 18 ft, not 30 ft.
- Keep the **panel-to-charger run as short as possible** (cost driver).
- Leave **clearance for car doors, storage, and the charger handle**.

## Step 1: Find Your Car's Charge Port

The single biggest variable. Common locations by manufacturer:

- **Tesla:** rear-driver corner.
- **Ford (Mustang Mach-E, F-150 Lightning):** front-driver fender.
- **Chevy (Bolt, Equinox EV, Blazer EV):** front-driver fender.
- **Rivian:** rear-driver corner.
- **Hyundai/Kia (Ioniq, EV6, EV9):** rear-passenger fender.
- **Nissan Leaf, Toyota bZ4X:** front-center nose.
- **VW ID.4:** rear-passenger fender.

If your garage parks the car nose-in (standard), a Tesla's charge port lands at the back wall. A Mach-E's lands at the front wall. Plan accordingly.

## Step 2: Decide Wall, Height, and Cable Length

Mount the charger on the wall closest to where the charge port will be when parked. The standard mount height is **48 inches** from the floor — within easy reach but above bumper height in case of a parking misjudgment.

Cable length: 20–25 ft is the right balance. Shorter cables (18 ft) are restrictive if you ever park backward or share the garage with a second car. Longer cables (30 ft) become heavy, harder to coil, and store badly.

For two-EV households or households that occasionally park outside the garage, install the charger near a corner so the cable can reach either bay or the driveway.

## Step 3: Plan Panel-to-Charger Run

This is the biggest install-cost variable. Shorter runs = less wire, less conduit, less labor. Specific patterns:

- **Panel in garage, charger in same garage:** simplest scope — shortest wire run, minimal conduit.
- **Panel in basement directly below garage:** straightforward run up through the floor or wall.
- **Panel in basement at opposite end of house:** longer wire run, more conduit, potentially finished spaces to navigate.
- **Panel in detached garage already:** same garage simple, but verify amperage capacity first.
- **Panel in main house, charger in detached garage:** the most involved — trenched feeder underground, separate grounding electrode, and potentially a sub-panel at the detached structure.

Every layout is quoted after a site visit so the number reflects your actual run distance and wall access, not a generic average.

If you have flexibility on charger location, prioritize the shortest panel-to-charger path that still serves the charge port well. ## Step 4: Account for the Charger's Own Footprint

Most Level 2 chargers are 8–14 inches wide and stick out 4–6 inches from the wall. Add the cable holster (3–5 inches deeper). Plan for:

- **24 inches of horizontal clearance** so you can plug/unplug without bending the cable sharply.
- **12 inches of vertical clearance** above the unit for venting on some models.
- **Door swing clearance** if mounted near a man-door.
- **Bumper distance** — keep the unit at least 12 inches back from where the car's front or rear bumper will sit.

## Step 5: Indoor vs Outdoor Considerations

Most West Michigan EV owners install inside an attached garage. If you need an exterior install:

- Use a **NEMA 4 or NEMA 4X rated unit** (most major brands have outdoor-rated SKUs).
- **Cover or canopy** extends charger life by reducing UV and ice exposure.
- **Cable becomes stiff below 0°F** — store the handle in a wall holster when not in use.
- **GFCI requirements** apply per NEC 625.54; outdoor installs must be protected.

Detached garages add a layer: feeders need underground conduit (typically Schedule 40 PVC at 24" depth), with grounding electrodes if the structure is treated as a separate building under NEC 250.32.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Installing too low.** A 36" mount looks fine until you crouch to plug in every night.
- **Installing in the back corner.** Looks tidy in an empty garage, but if you eventually park a second car or bike storage there, the charger becomes inaccessible.
- **Routing cable through walking paths.** A coiled cable across the floor becomes a tripping hazard the first time someone visits with kids.
- **Forgetting the second vehicle.** If household includes a non-EV second car, plan parking patterns now.
- **Hardwiring when you might move.** Plug-in (NEMA 14-50) installs are renter-friendly and let you take the charger with you. Hardwired wins on speed (48A) and clean aesthetics.

## Future-Proofing

If you have any plans to:

- Add a second EV
- Get solar
- Add battery backup
- Move the panel for a remodel

… mention it now. Stub-out conduit for a future second charger, or oversize the feeder, while the wall is open. Adding it during the original install is a fraction of the cost of pulling permits and opening walls again as a separate retrofit later.

## FAQs

**How high should an EV charger be mounted?**
48 inches from the floor is the residential standard. ADA-style installs go to 40 inches for accessibility. The key is whatever height makes plugging in effortless for the daily user.

**Can I install the charger outside my garage?**
Yes, with NEMA 4 or NEMA 4X rated equipment. Outdoor installs need GFCI protection and weatherproof boxes; cable stiffness in winter is a real issue without a covered location.

**What if my charger and panel are far apart?**
The install becomes more expensive (more wire, conduit, labor). If runs exceed 100 ft, voltage drop calculations may require larger wire than the breaker amperage would suggest.

**Should I get a plug-in or hardwired charger?**
Plug-in (NEMA 14-50) for flexibility and renters. Hardwired for max speed (48A) and clean look. Both are code-compliant when installed properly.

**Can two EVs share one charger?**
Functionally yes — most owners just swap plugs nightly. Some chargers (ChargePoint Home Flex, Wallbox Pulsar) support shared scheduling. Two cars on one charger only fails if both deplete simultaneously and need full charges overnight.
