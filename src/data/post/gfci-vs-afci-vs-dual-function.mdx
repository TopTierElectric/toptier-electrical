GFCI, AFCI, and dual-function breakers all interrupt power to protect you, but they detect different dangerous conditions. Knowing which is which (and which is required where) helps you understand what's happening when a breaker trips and what your home actually needs.

## Quick Answer

- **GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter)** — catches current leaking to ground (electric shock conditions). Required around water and outdoors.
- **AFCI (Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter)** — catches arcing in damaged or loose wiring (fire conditions). Required in living spaces.
- **Dual-function (DF)** — catches both. Increasingly common because most circuits now require both protections.

## What GFCI Protects Against

GFCI protection trips when current leaving on the hot wire doesn't equal current returning on the neutral — meaning some of it is going somewhere else. Usually that "somewhere else" is a person, water, or damaged insulation acting as an unintended path to ground.

GFCI saves lives in these scenarios:

- A hair dryer falls into a sink full of water
- A cord with damaged insulation contacts a grounded surface
- An appliance has internal moisture damage

GFCI is required by code in:

- Bathrooms (all receptacles)
- Kitchens (countertop receptacles, dishwasher)
- Laundry rooms
- Garages (all receptacles)
- Outdoor receptacles
- Crawl spaces and unfinished basements
- Pool, spa, hot tub equipment
- Within 6 feet of any sink

GFCI can be a receptacle (the kind with the test/reset buttons) or a breaker (in the panel). A GFCI breaker protects the entire circuit; a GFCI receptacle protects itself and any "downstream" receptacles wired through it.

## What AFCI Protects Against

AFCI looks for the unique electrical signature of an arc — the small fires that happen when wiring is damaged, connections are loose, or insulation is failing. Arcing produces a specific waveform that AFCI breakers detect.

AFCI prevents fires in these scenarios:

- A nail driven through a romex cable causing intermittent arcing
- A loose wire under a backstabbed receptacle terminal heating up
- An aging splice in an attic junction box
- A damaged extension cord pinched under furniture

AFCI is required by code (per NEC 210.12 in most modern adoptions) in:

- Bedrooms (all circuits)
- Living rooms, family rooms, dens
- Dining rooms
- Hallways
- Kitchens
- Laundry rooms
- Closets

In short: most living spaces. The intent is fire prevention in the rooms where you sleep and spend time.

## Dual Function Breakers

Many circuits now require both AFCI and GFCI protection. Rather than chain them or pick one, dual-function (DF) breakers do both in a single device.

When you'll see DF required:

- Kitchen receptacles (AFCI for general circuits + GFCI for countertop)
- Laundry circuits
- Some bathroom and basement circuits

Dual-function breakers cost more than standard or single-function breakers. The price difference is part of why modern code-compliant panel upgrades cost more than older replacements.

## How to Tell Which You Have

In the panel:

- **AFCI breaker** — has a small button labeled TEST. Often blue or another color stripe.
- **GFCI breaker** — also has a TEST button, often with a small light.
- **Dual-function breaker** — TEST button, sometimes labeled "AFCI/GFCI" or "DF".
- **Standard breaker** — no TEST button.

In a receptacle:

- **GFCI receptacle** — TEST and RESET buttons on the face, often a green or red light.

If a breaker trips and you don't see a TEST button, it's likely a standard overcurrent breaker. If there's a TEST button and the breaker keeps tripping, the protection is doing its job — there's a fault to find.

## What to Do If a Protected Breaker Keeps Tripping

Don't keep resetting it. Each trip can mean heat is building somewhere you can't see, or current is leaking somewhere it shouldn't be.

What an electrician checks:

- Total connected load on the circuit (overload?)
- Receptacles, switches, and junction boxes for damage or moisture (ground fault?)
- Wire condition for damage or arcing (arc fault?)
- The breaker itself (some brands have higher false-trip rates)
- Recent changes (new appliance, remodel, water intrusion, rodent activity)

Sometimes AFCI breakers do nuisance-trip on motor-driven equipment (vacuums, treadmills, certain power tools). That's not a real fault — but persistent tripping under multiple loads is worth investigating.

## When You Might Need a Code-Correction Project

Older homes often have circuits that don't meet today's GFCI/AFCI requirements. While existing wiring is grandfathered in most cases, two scenarios trigger required upgrades:

1. **Renovation or remodel** that opens walls or modifies circuits — those circuits typically must meet current code.
2. **Failed inspection** during a sale — the buyer's inspector flagged missing protection.

Code corrections in older homes are common scope and not as disruptive as a full rewire if approached correctly.

## FAQs

**Are AFCI receptacles a thing?**
Yes, but they're rare and usually limited to specific applications. AFCI is most commonly delivered via the breaker.

**Can I install GFCI/AFCI myself?**
Replacing a receptacle with a GFCI in the same location is within most DIY skill ranges if the wiring is simple. Breakers in a live panel are not — that's electrician work.

**My GFCI keeps tripping when I plug in a specific appliance. Bad GFCI?**
Probably not. The appliance is most likely leaking current. Try a different GFCI to confirm; if it also trips, the appliance is the problem.

**Do GFCI/AFCI breakers wear out?**
Yes, eventually. They're tested by the TEST button — if pressing TEST doesn't trip the breaker, it's failed and needs replacement.
