Knob-and-tube wiring (K&T) was the standard residential wiring method from roughly 1880 to 1940. Plenty of West Michigan homes still have it — entirely or in patches — and the question every owner of one eventually asks is some version of _"do I have to replace it, and what does that cost?"_ The honest answer is: it depends on what's connected to it, what's insulating it, and whether your insurance will write a policy at all.

## Quick Answer

- K&T is **not automatically unsafe** — it's safe in its original installed state.
- It becomes a hazard when **covered with insulation**, when **modified by amateurs**, or when **overloaded** by modern appliances.
- It has **no grounding conductor** — only two wires per circuit.
- Many insurers **decline or surcharge** policies on homes with active K&T.
- Full replacement runs $8,000–$25,000+ depending on home size and access.

## What K&T Actually Is

Knob-and-tube uses single-conductor copper wires running through open air, supported by porcelain knobs (for runs) and porcelain tubes (where wire passes through framing). The hot and neutral run separately, often a foot apart. Conductor insulation is rubberized cloth or, in later runs, varnished cambric.

Key characteristics:

- **No grounding conductor** — circuits are two-wire only.
- **Rated 60°C** — half the heat tolerance of modern 90°C wire.
- **Designed to dissipate heat in open air** — wrapping it in insulation defeats the design.
- **No sheathing** — physical damage is easier than with modern romex.

## When K&T Becomes a Real Problem

K&T failures usually trace to one of five causes, not the wire itself:

1. **Insulation blown over it.** Blown-in cellulose, fiberglass batts, or foam can trap heat in K&T and cause the rubberized insulation to degrade. This is the single biggest reason insurers refuse coverage.
2. **Amateur splices.** Generations of homeowners and unlicensed contractors have spliced modern wire onto K&T using wire nuts buried inside walls — illegal and a fire risk. We see this constantly in older Holland and Grand Rapids homes during renovations.
3. **Overloading.** A K&T circuit was designed for 1920s loads: a few lamps and maybe a radio. Plugging in a window AC, a microwave, and a space heater is asking the wire to dissipate heat it can't.
4. **No grounding.** Without a ground, three-prong outlets installed on K&T circuits are misleading (and code-violating) — appliances expecting a ground path don't have one.
5. **Damaged insulation.** Where wires pass through framing or near attic floor joists, mechanical damage or rodent activity can expose conductors.

## What to Do First (Before Touching Anything)

1. **Get an electrical inspection.** A licensed electrician can map your circuits, identify which are still K&T, and assess insulation contact. Plan on $200–$500 for a thorough panel-to-fixture inspection.
2. **Check insurance.** Call your homeowner's policy carrier. Some flat-out refuse K&T; others write at a surcharge; a few (like USAA on certain policies) treat it like any other wiring if inspected. Get this in writing before you decide.
3. **Decide partial vs full replacement.** Partial replacement makes sense for accessible runs (basement, attic, knob-and-tube in finished walls slated for remodel). Full replacement makes sense if you're renovating, refinancing, or selling.

## Partial vs Full Replacement: Cost and Scope

**Partial** ($3,000–$8,000): Replace K&T in accessible areas, leave intact runs in finished walls if they pass inspection. Common scope is basement and attic. Walls stay closed.

**Full** ($8,000–$25,000+): Replace all K&T with modern romex. Requires opening walls and ceilings. Best paired with a remodel or major renovation when walls are already open.

**Insurance-driven** ($5,000–$15,000): Replace whichever sections your insurer requires to keep the policy active, plus any amateur splices found during the work.

## Common Myths

**"K&T is illegal."** Not in Michigan. Existing K&T is grandfathered. New K&T installation is not allowed, but having it isn't a code violation.

**"All K&T houses fail inspection."** False. Home inspectors flag K&T but don't fail homes for having it. The actual outcome depends on the buyer, lender, and insurer.

**"I can just put romex in parallel."** No. Splicing modern wire to K&T using buried wire nuts is illegal and dangerous. Every connection must be in an accessible junction box.

**"Insulation can't touch K&T."** True for blown-in or batt insulation in direct contact. Some jurisdictions allow foam-insulated K&T if specifically inspected and signed off, but that's a narrow case.

## What an Electrician Looks For

During inspection, we open the panel and trace circuits, then pull a sample of outlets and switches in each major area to confirm wiring type. We check:

- Continuity of ground conductors (if claimed)
- Presence of amateur splices
- Insulation contact in attic and walls (where accessible)
- Visible deterioration of conductor insulation
- Overloaded circuits

## FAQs

**Is knob-and-tube wiring grandfathered in Michigan?**
Yes. Existing K&T is allowed to remain in service. New K&T installation is not permitted under the current Michigan Residential Code.

**Can I get homeowner's insurance with knob-and-tube?**
Some carriers refuse, some surcharge, a few cover it normally. The pattern across our customer base: get it in writing from your specific carrier before deciding scope.

**How long does it take to rewire a 2,000 sq ft house?**
Full rewire with modest demo: 2–4 weeks, depending on access and finishes. With walls already open during a renovation: 1–2 weeks.

**Can I leave K&T in walls if I rewire the rest?**
Sometimes — depends on the local AHJ (authority having jurisdiction) and your insurer. Abandoned K&T must be disconnected at both ends and clearly de-energized.

**Will rewiring increase my home's value?**
Buyers and appraisers consider K&T a known risk. Documentation of partial or full rewire typically supports value and smooths sales — though it rarely returns 1:1 on resale.
