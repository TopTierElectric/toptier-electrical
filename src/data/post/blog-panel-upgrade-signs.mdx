Your electrical panel is the command center for every circuit in your home. As devices, appliances, and EV chargers increase demand, older panels struggle to keep up. Here are the most common signs it may be time for a panel upgrade—and how to plan the next steps with confidence.

## 1) Breakers Trip Often

Occasional trips happen, but frequent resets are a red flag. If multiple circuits trip when you run the microwave, hair dryer, or HVAC, your panel may be overloaded. An upgrade adds capacity and stabilizes power delivery.

## 2) Your Panel Is 20–40 Years Old

Older panels weren't designed for today's electrical loads. If your panel uses fuses instead of breakers, or if the brand has known reliability concerns, it's worth evaluating. A newer panel provides better protection and space for modern circuit needs.

## 3) You're Adding an EV Charger or Generator

Level 2 EV chargers and standby generators require dedicated circuits with specific breaker sizing. If your panel doesn't have available space or capacity, an upgrade is typically part of the project scope. Planning this upfront avoids last-minute delays.

## 4) You're Doing a Major Renovation

Kitchen remodels, basement finishes, and home additions often require new circuits. If the panel is already near capacity, adding circuits isn't possible without an upgrade. It's best to evaluate the panel early in the planning process.

## 5) You Have Double-Tapped Breakers

A double-tapped breaker has two wires connected to a single breaker terminal. This is a code violation in most configurations and indicates the panel is out of space. An upgrade or sub-panel adds room to correct this safely.

## 6) You Smell Burning or See Scorch Marks

Burning odors near the panel or discoloration on the cover are serious warning signs. These indicate overheating, arcing, or a failing breaker. Shut off circuits if safe to do so and have an electrician inspect immediately.

## 7) The Lights Flicker When Appliances Start

Lights that dim or flicker when the AC, refrigerator, or washing machine starts can indicate a shared circuit problem or an undersized service. If this is a persistent issue across multiple areas of the house, the panel and overall service may need evaluation.

## What a Panel Upgrade Typically Involves

A service upgrade usually includes:

- Replacing the existing panel with a new, properly sized unit.
- Installing updated breakers for all circuits.
- Coordinating with the utility for a service reconnection (may require scheduling).
- Permit and inspection to confirm code compliance.

The [panel upgrades service page](/panel-upgrades) covers this in detail and explains what to expect during the process.

## When to Schedule an Evaluation

If you're experiencing any of the symptoms above, or if you're planning a project that will increase electrical demand, schedule an evaluation before the work begins. Understanding the current state of the panel helps scope the project accurately and avoids surprises.

## FAQs

**Is a panel that frequently trips breakers actually dangerous?**
Frequent trips are the breaker doing its job — interrupting an overload, short, or ground fault. The danger is in the cause: persistent overload on an undersized service, or breakers that have aged out and may fail to trip. Diagnosis first, fix second.

**How old is too old for an electrical panel?**
Most residential panels are designed for a 30–40 year service life. Federal Pacific Stab-Lok and Zinsco panels are flagged at any age due to documented failure modes. Panels from the 1970s–80s with no signs of damage may still be safe but are nearing the end of expected life.

**What are double-tapped breakers?**
Two wires landed under a breaker terminal that the manufacturer only listed for one. A common code violation that develops heat at the loose connection, then fails. Almost always a sign the panel is full and the real fix is a panel upgrade.

**Should I upgrade my panel before adding an EV charger or generator?**
Often yes. A load calculation tells whether the existing service supports the new equipment. If the panel is also at or near capacity, doing both at once is cheaper than two separate mobilizations.
