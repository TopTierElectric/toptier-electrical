For a long time, 200-amp service was the default for new homes. It's still right for most. But homes are getting bigger, electric loads are getting heavier, and a growing number of homeowners are asking whether 400-amp is worth it. The answer depends on what the home actually pulls—not the square footage on the listing.

## Quick Answer

**200-amp service** comfortably runs a typical home with:

- Standard HVAC (gas heat, central AC)
- Electric range, dryer, water heater (or some on gas)
- One Level 2 EV charger
- Modest exterior loads
- Reasonable lighting and receptacle loads

**400-amp service** starts to make sense when:

- The home has all-electric heat (heat pump or resistance) plus modern electrical loads
- You're running two EV chargers, especially at 48A each
- You have a shop or accessory dwelling unit on the same service
- You have a large standby generator integrated into the home
- Your load calculation comes back at 80%+ of 200A and you have room to grow
- The home is over ~5,000 sq ft with electrified everything

A real load calc gives you the right answer for your home, not a square-foot rule of thumb.

## What 200-Amp Service Typically Supports

A typical 200-amp residential service can handle:

- HVAC (compressor, air handler, electric heat strips for backup)
- Electric range and oven
- Electric dryer
- Electric water heater
- Dishwasher, microwave, refrigerator, garbage disposal
- General lighting and receptacle loads
- One Level 2 EV charger (40A typical, sometimes 48A with load management)
- A modest workshop or detached garage subpanel

That's a lot. Most homes never come close to maxing it out, even with electric appliances throughout.

## When 400-Amp Service May Make Sense

The case for 400-amp gets stronger as electric loads stack up:

- **Two EV chargers** at 48A each on a continuous-load basis
- **All-electric home** with a heat pump (and resistance backup), induction range, electric dryer, electric water heater
- **Detached shop or ADU** with its own meaningful loads
- **Standby generator** sized to back up much of the home, not just essentials
- **Large pool/spa equipment** combined with the above
- **Future-proofing** for a remodel or expansion that's coming in 2-3 years

Builders often install 400-amp service in custom homes because adding capacity later is expensive and disruptive. If you're at 75% of 200A on the load calc and adding more, the cost-benefit shifts toward doing it once.

## Breaker Spaces vs Service Capacity

A 400-amp service typically uses one of two configurations:

1. **One 400-amp panel** with high circuit count
2. **Two 200-amp panels** off a 400-amp service (common—each panel handles a portion of the home)

The two-panel approach gives you cleaner separation (e.g., one panel for the main house, one for the shop or ADU) and often more total breaker spaces than a single panel.

Capacity is set by the service entrance, meter base, and main breaker. Breaker space is set by the panel(s) you choose. They're related but separate decisions.

## Cost and Scope Differences

A 200A → 400A service upgrade involves:

- New service entrance conductors (larger gauge)
- New meter base (often a 400A meter base or two 200A meter bases)
- New service mast or underground service
- New panel(s) and breakers
- Grounding system update to current code
- Utility coordination for service drop or feeder change
- Permit and inspection

That's more scope than a panel replacement. The cost difference between a 200A panel replacement and a 400A service upgrade can be substantial. The right question isn't "how much does each cost?" It's "which one solves the actual problem?"

## Why Load Calculation Matters

NEC Article 220 gives the standard method for calculating residential service load. The optional methods (220.83 for existing dwellings, 220.87 for max demand from history) are often more accurate for real homes because they account for diversity—the fact that not everything runs at once.

A load calc tells you:

- The current calculated load
- Headroom on the existing service
- Whether new loads (EV, heat pump, generator) fit
- Whether a panel replacement, panel + service upgrade, or full 400A service is the right call

Skipping the calc and guessing usually results in either over-spending on capacity you don't need or under-sizing into a problem.

## Planning for EV Chargers and Generators

Two of the biggest drivers of service upgrade conversations:

**EV chargers.** One charger at 40A on a 200A service is fine for most homes. Two chargers at 48A each, especially with electric heat or AC, push the math. Load management can sometimes solve this without a service upgrade, but if more loads are coming, the upgrade is the cleaner answer.

**Standby generators.** A large standby generator (20kW+ air-cooled, or liquid-cooled) feeding most of the home through an automatic transfer switch sometimes needs reconfiguration that's easier with 400A service. Smaller, essential-loads-only generators don't drive this decision.

## FAQs

**Can I upgrade from 100A to 200A or 200A to 400A?**
Yes. Both are common upgrade paths. Scope and cost depend on the existing service, mast, meter base, and grounding.

**Will my utility have to change the service drop?**
Usually yes for a 200A → 400A upgrade. We coordinate with the utility.

**Do I need 400-amp service for an EV charger?**
Almost never for one charger. Maybe for two high-amperage chargers plus heavy electric loads.

**Is 400-amp service overkill for a typical home?**
For most homes, yes. For all-electric homes with two EVs, a heat pump, a shop, and a standby generator, it's appropriate.
