A whole-home generator and a portable generator solve the same problem — keeping the lights on when the grid goes down — but they cost different amounts, install differently, and protect different loads. For most West Michigan homeowners, the right choice depends on outage frequency, must-run loads, and how willing you are to drag a 200-pound unit out of the garage in a January ice storm.

## Quick Answer

- **Whole-home (standby):** $8,000–$18,000 installed, runs automatically, powers everything, fueled by propane or natural gas.
- **Portable:** $600–$3,500, manual setup, powers select circuits, runs on gasoline.
- Choose whole-home if you have well/sump pumps, work from home, or have medical equipment.
- Choose portable if outages are rare and you only need fridge + a few lights.
- Either way, you need a transfer switch or interlock — never backfeed.

## Where Each One Wins

### Whole-home wins if you have:

- **A well or sump pump.** Both are critical loads in West Michigan — wells for drinking water, sumps for keeping basements dry. Neither can wait for you to refuel a portable.
- **Frequent or long outages.** Lakeshore communities (Holland, Grand Haven, Saugatuck) lose power to wind events more often than inland areas.
- **A medical device.** Oxygen concentrators, CPAP machines, dialysis equipment need uninterrupted power.
- **A home office.** Two-day outages cost more in lost work than the install premium.
- **An EV charger.** Whole-home generators can keep an EV at usable charge during multi-day outages.

### Portable wins if you have:

- **Rare outages** (a few hours, once or twice a year).
- **Gas heat with no AC backup needed in summer.**
- **A small must-run list:** fridge, a few lights, furnace blower, modem.
- **Storage space** and the physical ability to set it up in bad weather.

## What Both Need: A Transfer Switch or Interlock

You cannot legally or safely run a generator by plugging it into a wall outlet ("backfeeding"). That sends electricity back onto the grid and can kill the lineman trying to restore your power. Every generator install — portable or standby — requires one of:

- **Automatic transfer switch (ATS):** standard with whole-home generators; switches automatically on outage detection.
- **Manual transfer switch:** for portables; you flip selected circuits over by hand.
- **Generator interlock kit:** a code-approved sliding bracket on the panel that prevents the main breaker and generator breaker from being on at the same time.

Interlock kits are the most economical legal option for portables and are common in Holland and Hudsonville installs. They still require a licensed electrician and a permit.

## Fuel Considerations in Michigan

Propane and natural gas (whole-home) win hands-down for long outages: no refueling, no carrying cans, no shelf-life issues. Natural gas is the best option if your home is plumbed for it. Otherwise, a 500-gallon propane tank gives a 22 kW unit roughly 4 days of runtime.

Gasoline (portables) becomes a logistics problem during multi-day events: cans go empty, station pumps lose power, and old gas in storage gums up carburetors. If you go portable, use fuel stabilizer year-round.

## When to Call a Pro

A licensed electrician should evaluate your panel and load profile before you spend on either type of unit. ## FAQs

**Can I install a whole-home generator myself?**
No. Michigan requires a licensed electrician for the transfer switch and panel tie-in, and a licensed plumber or gas fitter for fuel line work. Permits and inspections are mandatory.

**How loud is a standby generator running?**
Modern units run at 60–67 dB at 23 feet — about the level of a dishwasher. Portables are typically 70–80 dB and noticeably louder.

**Will a portable generator hurt my electronics?**
Quality inverter portables (Honda, Yamaha, Generac iQ) produce clean sine-wave power safe for electronics. Cheap open-frame units can produce harmonic distortion that damages sensitive gear.

**Can a whole-home generator power an electric car?**
A 22 kW unit can run a Level 2 charger at reduced amperage during an outage. You'll need a smart load manager to prevent overload when AC and the charger run simultaneously.

**How long do whole-home generators last?**
20–25 years with annual maintenance is typical. The transfer switch usually outlasts the engine.
