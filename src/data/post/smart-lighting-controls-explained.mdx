Smart lighting controls used to mean app-only switches that lost connectivity every other week. The category has matured: today's smart dimmers, scene controllers, and occupancy sensors are reliable wired devices that integrate with HomeKit, Alexa, Google Home, and Matter. The hard part isn't whether they work — it's choosing which ones are actually worth installing versus marketing fluff. Here is the practical breakdown.

## Quick Answer

- **Smart dimmer:** replaces existing dimmer; adds remote, scheduling, and dim curves.
- **Scene controller:** single button triggers a saved lighting state across multiple fixtures.
- **Occupancy sensor:** lights on when you enter, off when room is empty.
- **Vacancy sensor:** manual on, auto off (more efficient than occupancy).
- Best results come from **hardwired devices, not smart bulbs in dumb sockets**.

## The Four Devices Worth Installing

### 1. Smart Dimmers

A smart dimmer replaces a standard dimmer or switch. It controls the load directly and integrates with your home network for app control, voice control, and scheduling. Common reliable brands: Lutron Caséta, Leviton Decora Smart, Lutron RA3 (premium), GE Cync.

Key spec: confirm the dimmer is rated for your LED load. Most modern smart dimmers handle 150–300 W of LED. Mismatches cause flickering and humming.

### 2. Scene Controllers

A scene controller is a button panel — usually 4 or 5 buttons in place of a single switch — that triggers preset lighting states. "Movie" turns down living room lights to 20% and porch off. "Morning" turns on kitchen at 60% and entry at 100%. Lutron's Pico remote and Caséta scene controllers are the residential standard.

Scene controllers shine in kitchens (3+ fixtures), living rooms, and entries. They're overkill for single-fixture rooms.

### 3. Occupancy Sensors

Lights on when motion is detected; lights off after a delay (typically 5–15 minutes) when no motion. Best for:

- **Bathrooms** (lights left on overnight)
- **Closets** (manual switch always forgotten)
- **Pantries**
- **Mudrooms**
- **Stairwells**

Skip occupancy sensors in spaces where you sit still (dining rooms, home offices) — the lights cut off when you stop moving for the delay window.

### 4. Vacancy Sensors

Same hardware as occupancy sensors, different logic: **manual on, auto off**. You hit the switch when you enter; lights turn off automatically when you leave. This is more energy-efficient because lights don't turn on when you don't need them (walking past the door, retrieving something quickly).

Vacancy sensors are the default code requirement in some commercial spaces under ASHRAE 90.1. Increasingly common in residential too.

## Smart Dimmers vs Smart Bulbs

The biggest decision in this category. Two paths:

**Smart dimmer + standard bulbs**:

- One device per circuit (one switch).
- Works with any LED, halogen, or incandescent bulb.
- Failure mode: dimmer fails, replace one device.
- Cost: device + install per dimmer; bulbs are normal cost. Quoted per scope.

**Smart bulbs + standard switch**:

- Bulbs are individually addressable (color, scene per bulb).
- Switch must stay ON or bulbs lose power and "forget" they're smart.
- Family members flipping the wall switch break the whole system.
- Cost: smart bulbs × number of fixtures per room — scales with bulb count.

For 90% of households, smart dimmers are the right call. Smart bulbs make sense for color-changing accent fixtures, lamps, or single-bulb fixtures where wall-switch control isn't practical.

## Wiring Realities

Most smart switches and dimmers require a **neutral wire** at the switch box. Older homes (pre-1980) often have switch loops with no neutral — just hot and switched-hot. Options:

1. **Lutron Caséta** — works with no neutral, the only major-brand reliable option for older homes.
2. **Run a neutral.** Possible if wall access is available during a remodel.
3. **Use smart bulbs instead.** Sidesteps the neutral problem.

For multi-way switches (3-way, 4-way), most smart dimmers need a specific compatible remote on the other end. Lutron Caséta and RA3 both have Pico remote pairings; Leviton has companion switches.

## Integration Stacks That Actually Work

**Lutron Caséta** (Smart Bridge Pro):

- Most reliable wireless lighting system.
- Works with HomeKit, Alexa, Google, SmartThings.
- Plays well with both Picos and third-party scene controllers.
- Limitation: closed protocol, slower to add new device types.

**Lutron RA3** (residential premium):

- Hardwired-first, with wireless backup.
- Full home scenes, sun-tracking schedules.
- Higher cost but architect-grade reliability.

**Matter-over-Thread** (newer, multi-brand):

- Open standard supported by Apple, Google, Samsung, Amazon.
- Mix-and-match brands without vendor lock-in.
- Earlier-generation devices still working out reliability.

## What Doesn't Work As Well As Advertised

- **Voice-only control as the primary interface.** Works great until the network's down or guests visit.
- **Color-changing as a daily-driver feature.** Most households set whites and forget about colors after a month.
- **Mobile-app-only switches** (no physical button). Frustrating for visitors and during outages.
- **Wi-Fi-only smart bulbs.** Network outages break them; battery-life concerns on wireless mesh alternatives.

## When to Call a Pro

Anytime you're touching a switch box that doesn't have a neutral, or installing more than 5–6 devices that need to integrate. The wiring is straightforward; the network and scene programming is what takes time to get right. ## FAQs

**Do smart dimmers work with LED bulbs?**
Yes, when both are compatible. Confirm the dimmer is rated "LED compatible" and the bulb is marked "dimmable." Mismatches cause flicker, humming, or limited dimming range.

**Can I install a smart switch myself?**
Replacing a switch is within most DIY skill ranges if you turn off the breaker, confirm dead with a tester, and follow the manufacturer wiring diagram. Anything beyond single-switch replacement (multi-way, no-neutral wiring) usually benefits from an electrician.

**What's the difference between a smart switch and a smart dimmer?**
A smart switch is on/off only; a smart dimmer can vary brightness. Use dimmers for living spaces and switches for fans, exhausts, or outlets.

**Will smart switches keep working during a power outage?**
The switch itself stores state, but if the load (lights) has no power, nothing changes visibly. When power returns, the switch resumes its last state.

**Do occupancy sensors save much electricity?**
In high-traffic, often-forgotten spaces (bathrooms, closets, garages) they cut lighting energy 30–70%. Negligible in spaces already managed by manual switches.
