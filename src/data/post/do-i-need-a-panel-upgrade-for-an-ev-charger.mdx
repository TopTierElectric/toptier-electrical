Not always. Some homes have plenty of capacity for a properly installed Level 2 charger. Others need load management, panel work, or a full service upgrade before the charger goes in. The right answer depends on what your existing electrical service can actually deliver—not just what's printed on the panel cover.

## Quick Answer

If your home has a 200-amp service, modern wiring, and reasonable existing loads, a Level 2 EV charger usually fits without a panel upgrade. If you're on 100-amp service, the panel is full, the panel is aging, or the home has electric heat, AC, and a hot tub already pulling hard, a panel or service upgrade becomes more likely. A licensed electrician confirms this with a load calculation, not a guess.

## What an Electrician Checks Before Installing an EV Charger

A safe install starts with five questions:

- **Service size.** What's the main breaker rating—100A, 150A, 200A, or 400A?
- **Existing load.** What does your home already pull on a hot day with everything running?
- **Charger amperage.** Are you installing a 30-amp, 40-amp, 48-amp, or 50-amp circuit?
- **Breaker space.** Is there physical room in the panel for a two-pole breaker?
- **Panel condition.** Is the panel modern, properly grounded, and code-compliant?

A load calculation per NEC Article 220 (or the optional method in 220.83/220.87) tells us whether the charger fits. We don't skip that step.

## Breaker Space Is Not the Same as Capacity

This trips up a lot of homeowners. A 200-amp panel with two open slots may still be fully loaded based on the math. Conversely, a panel that looks crowded may have plenty of headroom because some circuits are oversized for what they actually run. Empty slots only matter if the service can support the new load. Capacity comes first, breaker space second.

## Hardwired vs Plug-In EV Charger

Both work. The right choice depends on your goals:

- **Hardwired chargers** look cleaner, allow higher current (48A and above usually require hardwiring), and don't add a receptacle that can degrade over time under continuous load.
- **Plug-in chargers** (NEMA 14-50 receptacle) give you portability if you move and can simplify replacement, but the receptacle itself becomes a wear point. Use a high-quality industrial-grade receptacle and the right GFCI protection.

For most homes that plan to keep the charger long-term, hardwired is the simpler choice.

## When Load Management May Be Better Than a Service Upgrade

Load management hardware (sometimes called dynamic load balancing or smart splitters) lets you add a Level 2 charger to a panel that wouldn't otherwise have headroom. It works by monitoring whole-home demand and throttling the charger when other loads spike.

This is often cheaper than a full service upgrade, especially when:

- The service entrance, meter base, and grounding are already healthy
- The panel has space but the load calc is borderline
- You charge overnight when other loads are low anyway

It's not always the right call. If the panel is aging, the service is undersized for everything else (not just the charger), or you're planning more upgrades soon, fixing the underlying capacity is usually the better long-term move.

## When a Panel Upgrade or Service Upgrade May Be Needed

Panel or service work is more likely when:

- The home has 100-amp service and significant existing electric loads
- The panel is full, double-tapped, or has known problem brands
- You're charging at 48A and the load calc doesn't support it
- You're also planning EV #2, a generator, an addition, a hot tub, or HVAC changes
- The service mast, meter base, or grounding doesn't meet current code

In those cases, the panel upgrade is doing real work—creating capacity, modernizing the system, and giving you breaker space for what's coming next.

## Cost and Scope Factors

EV charger projects price out based on:

- Distance and route from panel to charger location
- Wall finish (drywall, masonry, exterior siding) and conduit needs
- Service size and panel condition
- Whether load management or a panel/service upgrade is required
- Permit and inspection coordination

Charger cost itself varies, but the install scope is what tends to shift the total most.

## FAQs

**Can I just plug a Level 2 charger into a dryer outlet?**
Sometimes, briefly—but it's not a real install. The dryer circuit is shared, the receptacle isn't rated for continuous load, and you lose the dryer when charging. A dedicated circuit is the right answer.

**Will a 100-amp service work for an EV charger?**
Sometimes, with a smaller charger and a load calc that confirms capacity. With electric heat or a heat pump, usually not without load management or an upgrade.

**Do I need a permit?**
Most jurisdictions require one. We coordinate the permit and inspection.

**How long does the install take?**
Most charger installs are a single visit once the plan is confirmed. Panel or service upgrades add a day and a planned outage window.
