LED retrofits and full fixture replacements both have a place. The right call depends on the existing fixture's condition, rating, location, brightness needs, and how long you plan to keep it.

## Quick Answer

Retrofit when:

- The fixture is in good condition, properly rated, and in a workable location
- You want a quick, low-disruption upgrade
- The retrofit kit fits cleanly without exceeding the fixture's wattage rating

Replace when:

- The fixture is aged, damaged, or no longer rated for its location
- You want different beam spread, color temperature, or output than the original allows
- The layout is wrong (wrong spacing, wrong location, wrong type)
- You're consolidating switching, dimming, or controls
- The fixture is in a high-bay, exterior, or wet location where rating matters more

## When an LED Retrofit Makes Sense

LED retrofits replace just the bulb or the bulb + driver inside an existing housing. The housing, trim, and wiring stay. They work well when:

- **The fixture is healthy.** Clean housing, good gasket (in damp/wet locations), wiring intact.
- **The retrofit is properly rated.** Insulation contact (IC), wet-location, dimmable—match the original or better.
- **You're staying with the same look.** The retrofit fits the existing trim and aperture.
- **You want minimal disruption.** No drywall work, no wiring changes, no commissioning.

Common retrofit wins: kitchen recessed cans where the layout is fine, hallway and closet fixtures, basic decorative fixtures with healthy housings.

## When Fixture Replacement Is Better

Replacement makes more sense when:

- **The fixture is old or damaged.** Brittle insulation, broken sockets, corroded contacts. Pretty bulbs in a bad housing don't fix the housing.
- **The rating is wrong for the location.** Damp/wet ratings matter. Insulation-contact (IC) matters in insulated ceilings. Outdoor fixtures degrade.
- **The layout is wrong.** Two cans where you needed four, or a single fixture lighting an island that needs better task light.
- **You want better optics.** Beam spread, glare control, and color rendering vary widely. A retrofit can only do so much inside the original housing.
- **You're upgrading control.** Going from on/off to dimming, smart switches, or 0-10V dimming may require fixture-level changes.
- **High-bay, shop, or exterior.** Retrofits exist, but in high-output spaces, purpose-built LED fixtures usually outperform retrofits in efficiency and longevity.

## Brightness Is Not Just Wattage

The old habit was "60-watt bulb" or "100-watt bulb." LEDs make wattage misleading because output per watt varies by product. Look at:

- **Lumens** (total light output)
- **Beam angle** (narrow spot vs wide flood)
- **CCT (color temperature)**—2700K warm, 3000K soft, 4000K neutral, 5000K cool
- **CRI (color rendering)**—80+ is acceptable, 90+ is noticeably better in kitchens, bathrooms, and finished spaces

A "100-watt equivalent" LED can deliver anywhere from 1,400 to 1,800+ lumens depending on the fixture. Match output and color to the room, not the old wattage.

## Safety and Rating Considerations

This is where retrofits go wrong:

- **Wet/damp location ratings.** A retrofit kit must match or exceed the original fixture's rating, especially outdoors and in bathrooms.
- **IC (insulation contact) ratings.** If the housing is in contact with insulation, the retrofit must be IC-rated.
- **Total wattage.** LED retrofits draw less power than incandescents, so this is rarely a problem—but it matters in fixtures that were already at the limit.
- **Dimmer compatibility.** Old incandescent dimmers often don't work with LEDs and cause flicker, hum, or premature driver failure. Match dimmer to driver.
- **Driver location.** External drivers need ventilation and access. Burying a driver inside a sealed soffit shortens its life.

When in doubt, replace the fixture with a purpose-built LED rather than retrofitting around a rating mismatch.

## Lighting Layout Matters

The biggest gain from a lighting upgrade often isn't LED itself—it's better layout. Recessed cans spaced too far apart leave dark corners. Fixtures aimed at walls instead of work surfaces leave kitchens feeling dim. Exterior fixtures in the wrong location create glare without illuminating walking paths.

A real lighting plan considers:

- Task surfaces (counters, desks, workbenches)
- Ambient light levels (general room comfort)
- Accent (artwork, focal points)
- Switching logic (where you turn it on, where you turn it off)
- Dimming zones (separate the cans from the pendants from the under-cabinet)

Sometimes the right answer is "retrofit these, replace those, add a new circuit for the third group." That's fine. The plan should match how you use the space.

## Cost and Scope Factors

- Number of fixtures and access (ceiling height, attic vs slab construction)
- Whether wiring or switching changes are needed
- Driver and dimmer compatibility
- Specialty fixtures (high-bay, outdoor, wet location)
- Finish work after fixture replacement

Retrofits are usually quicker and cheaper per fixture. Replacements give you better long-term performance and flexibility.

## FAQs

**Will my existing dimmer work with LEDs?**
Sometimes. Old incandescent dimmers often need replacement to match the LED driver. We confirm compatibility before installing.

**Can I mix retrofits and full replacements in the same project?**
Yes—often the smartest approach. Healthy fixtures get retrofits; problem locations get new fixtures.

**How do I avoid flicker?**
Match dimmer and driver, use quality fixtures with reputable drivers, and verify dimming range during install.

**Does color temperature really matter?**
Yes. Mixing 2700K and 4000K in the same room looks wrong. Pick a temperature per room and stay consistent.
