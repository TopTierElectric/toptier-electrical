Cost is the first thing most homeowners ask about a panel upgrade — and unfortunately, "average" numbers online are misleading because the project has a wide scope range. Two homes that both "need a panel upgrade" can have totally different bills based on what the work actually involves.

## Quick Answer

Most residential panel upgrades fall into one of three buckets:

- **Panel replacement only** (existing service stays the same size): swap the panel and breakers, keep the meter base, mast, and service entrance conductors. Usually the lowest-cost option in the panel-upgrade family.
- **Panel + service upgrade** (e.g., 100A → 200A): replace panel, breakers, meter base, service entrance conductors, sometimes the mast. Adds utility coordination and a planned outage. Costs more, takes longer.
- **400A service upgrade**: full reconfiguration with larger conductors, often a 400A meter base or two 200A panels. Highest cost, most coordination — but the right answer for all-electric homes with EV, generator, heat pump, and shop loads.

A real load calculation tells you which bucket fits your home. Skipping the calculation and guessing usually costs more in the long run.

## What Drives the Cost

### 1. Service Size and Scope

The biggest cost driver is whether you need a panel replacement, a service upgrade, or a full 400A service. Each step up adds materials and labor:

- Larger conductors and breakers
- Bigger meter base
- Possibly a new mast
- More utility coordination
- Larger or multiple panels

### 2. Existing Panel Condition

A clean swap of a healthy older panel takes less time than tearing out a corroded panel with melted insulation, double-tapped breakers, and unlabeled circuits. The condition you start from sets the labor.

### 3. Mast, Meter Base, and Grounding

If the mast is leaning, the meter base is corroded, or the grounding doesn't meet current code, those have to be addressed at the same time. They're often bundled into a service upgrade quote because doing them separately later is more expensive.

### 4. Permit and Utility Coordination

Permits are required and inspection is scheduled. Service upgrades that involve a utility disconnect/reconnect add scheduling time. The labor for this is usually included in the quote — but in jurisdictions with stricter requirements, it can add up.

### 5. Drywall and Finish Work

If the panel is staying in the same location, finish work is minimal. If the panel is moving (often the case when an electrical room is being reorganized or when an old panel is in a closet that's being remodeled), drywall, paint, and trim work is part of the scope.

### 6. AFCI/GFCI Breakers

Modern code requires arc-fault and ground-fault protection on most circuits. Code-required AFCI/GFCI breakers cost more than standard breakers. A 30-circuit panel with full code-compliant protection costs more than a 30-circuit panel with all standard breakers.

### 7. Add-On Loads

If the panel work is bundled with EV charger installation, generator transfer switch, hot tub circuit, or HVAC dedicated circuits, those are included in the same project quote. Bundling reduces total cost vs. separate visits.

## What Doesn't Drive the Cost (Much)

- **Brand of panel.** Reputable brands (Square D, Eaton, Siemens) are similarly priced. Choose based on availability and breaker compatibility, not price.
- **Color.** Cosmetic.
- **Smart panel features.** They exist (load monitoring, app integration), but most homes don't need them. Adding smart features bumps cost meaningfully without proportional benefit for most use cases.

## How to Compare Quotes

Apples-to-apples means asking each electrician:

1. What's the scope — panel replacement only, or panel + service?
2. What service size is being installed (100A, 150A, 200A, 400A)?
3. Are AFCI/GFCI breakers included per current code?
4. Is the mast / meter base / grounding included if needed?
5. Are permits and inspection included?
6. Is drywall and finish work included if the panel is moving?
7. Is utility coordination included?
8. What's the planned outage window?

If two quotes have a big price gap, the answers to those questions usually explain it.

## Why "Cheap" Quotes Often Cost More Later

The two most common ways panel upgrades go wrong:

- **Skipped scope** — quote was for "just the panel" but the mast / meter base / grounding turn out to need work, and now you're paying for change orders.
- **Missing AFCI/GFCI** — quote left them off to come in lower, and the inspector requires them. Now the breakers get added at extra cost.

A clear scope before work begins avoids both. If a quote looks low and doesn't itemize what's included, it's worth asking.

## FAQs

**How long does a panel upgrade take?**
Most residential panel replacements finish in a single day. Service upgrades that involve the utility add scheduling time and may take longer due to outage coordination.

**Will my power be off?**
Yes — there's a planned outage window during the work. Most of the day, in most cases.

**Do I need a permit?**
Yes. We coordinate the permit and inspection.

**Can I keep the same panel location?**
Usually yes. If clearances and code requirements are met, it's the most cost-effective approach.
