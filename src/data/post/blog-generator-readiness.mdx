When storms roll through West Michigan, power outages can last from a few hours to several days. A standby generator or portable backup plan keeps your family comfortable, protects appliances, and preserves food. Use this checklist each season to make sure your backup power plan is ready when you need it.

## 1) Test Your Generator Monthly

Run standby units according to the manufacturer's schedule, usually once per month. Listen for unusual noises, check the status lights, and confirm the unit transitions correctly when utility power is interrupted.

## 2) Verify Fuel and Battery Health

Standby generators run on propane or natural gas. Confirm the supply is connected and at sufficient pressure. For portable units, use fresh fuel and add a stabilizer if the generator hasn't been used recently. Check the battery on auto-start models—a dead start battery is a common failure point.

## 3) Inspect the Transfer Switch

The transfer switch is what isolates your home from the utility grid and connects the generator safely. Check that the enclosure is secure, connections are tight, and there are no visible signs of arcing or corrosion. If you're unsure about the condition of the transfer switch, schedule an inspection before storm season.

## 4) Review Your Load List

Know which circuits or appliances your generator is sized to support. Most standby units can't power everything in the house at once. Prioritize: sump pump, HVAC blower, refrigerator, lights, and medical equipment. Understanding your load helps avoid overloading the generator during an outage.

## 5) Check the Generator Location

Generators must be installed and operated outdoors, away from windows, doors, and vents. If your generator is in a detached structure or has an outdoor enclosure, confirm the area is clear of debris. Verify exhaust flow isn't directed toward any occupied area.

## 6) Confirm Exercise Mode Is Active

Standby generators have an automatic exercise mode that runs the unit for a short period on a scheduled basis. Verify that exercise mode is enabled and note when it last ran. This keeps the engine in good condition and confirms the auto-start function is working.

## 7) Schedule Preventive Maintenance

Annual service includes checking oil level, replacing spark plugs and air filter, testing battery voltage, and inspecting the fuel system. Many manufacturers recommend a tune-up every 12 months or after a certain number of operating hours. A well-maintained generator will start when it matters most.

## 8) Have an Electrician Inspect Before Storm Season

If you've had the generator for several years without a professional inspection, schedule one before the next storm season. An electrician can verify transfer switch condition, check wiring and connections, confirm code compliance, and note any changes in your home's load that might affect generator sizing.
