A restaurant electrical build-out is one of the most demanding commercial scopes in residential-and-light-commercial work. You're combining heavy kitchen loads, hood and refrigeration motors, code-driven safety systems, point-of-sale infrastructure, and customer-facing lighting — all on a fixed-date opening schedule that ties into health-department, fire-marshal, and AHJ approvals. Here is what we plan for on West Michigan restaurant fit-outs, and what owners and contractors should expect.

## Quick Answer

- **Service size:** most full-service restaurants need **400 amp or higher** 3-phase service.
- **Permit timeline:** allow **4–8 weeks** for plan review and final inspections.
- **Hood interlocks** must shut down cooking equipment on exhaust failure (per NFPA 96).
- **Walk-in refrigeration** needs dedicated circuits with alarms.
- **GFCI everywhere** there's water or potential water — extensive in commercial kitchens.

## Service Sizing

The biggest first-decision item. Loads stack fast:

- **Kitchen equipment:** range, ovens, fryers, salamanders, dish machine — easily 80–150 amps combined.
- **Refrigeration:** walk-in compressors, line refrigeration, ice machine — 30–60 amps.
- **Hood system:** exhaust fan, makeup air, controls — 15–30 amps.
- **HVAC:** rooftop or split units sized for full-house — 30–80 amps.
- **Lighting:** dining + kitchen + back-of-house — 15–30 amps.
- **Receptacles:** POS, prep, miscellaneous — 30–50 amps.

Even a small fast-casual concept totals 250–350 amps connected. Full-service with bar, walk-ins, and large kitchen rapidly hits 400+ amps 3-phase. Pulling service is often the long-lead item: 6–12 weeks with the utility on a new service drop.

## Required Code Items

### Hood interlock (NFPA 96)

The exhaust hood system must interlock with cooking equipment so that loss of exhaust shuts off gas or electric cooking sources. This is wired through a control panel and inspected by both fire marshal and AHJ.

### Ansul / fire suppression interlocks

The hood's wet-chemical suppression system must shut off gas and electric to all hood-protected appliances when discharged. Coordination with the suppression contractor is critical — they install the pull station and links; we wire the shunt trip and microswitches.

### Emergency egress lighting

Battery-backed lighting on every egress path, sized for 90 minutes of operation. Required in every restaurant regardless of occupancy load. Exit signs at all doors with appropriate viewing distance.

### GFCI protection

Per NEC 210.8(B), GFCI protection is required in all commercial kitchen, food prep, and dishwashing areas. Most outlets in a restaurant kitchen are GFCI-required.

### Disconnects per appliance

Each fixed kitchen appliance (range, oven, dish machine, mixers) needs a disconnect within sight. This is a major source of code corrections during inspection — easily missed in original install.

### Walk-in safety systems

Walk-in freezers and coolers require interior emergency lighting, panic-release door hardware, and alarm/temperature monitoring with audible warning at temperature breach.

### Energy code compliance

ASHRAE 90.1 or local energy code applies. Lighting power density limits, occupancy controls in back-of-house, and daylight harvesting in zones with windows are all standard requirements.

## Permitting in Michigan

A restaurant build-out typically requires:

- **Building permit** (architect-stamped drawings).
- **Electrical permit** (load calculation, panel schedule, hood control documentation).
- **Mechanical permit** (HVAC and exhaust).
- **Plumbing permit.**
- **Health department review** (separate from building).
- **Fire marshal review** (egress, exit signs, suppression interlock).

Plan review can run 4–8 weeks depending on AHJ and completeness of drawings. The fastest projects we work on have all sub-contractors coordinated before drawings are submitted; the slowest are where each trade discovers conflicts during construction.

## What Goes Wrong (and How to Avoid It)

### Underestimated service

Tenant takes a space "with 200 amp service available" and discovers their kitchen needs 350 amps. Now the utility has to upgrade the service drop — 6–12 weeks of delay.

**Avoid:** load calculation during lease negotiation, not after lease signing.

### Hood disconnect schedule conflict

The hood manufacturer's shutdown sequence doesn't match the kitchen equipment's startup logic. Equipment trips mid-service.

**Avoid:** coordinate with hood and suppression contractors during electrical rough-in, not at final inspection.

### Walk-in alarm not interlocked

Walk-in goes warm overnight, no notification, thousands of dollars of food spoiled.

**Avoid:** confirm alarm wiring includes both audible local alarm and remote notification (phone or POS system tie-in).

### POS circuit shared with kitchen loads

POS reboots during the dinner rush because a fryer or mixer kicks on a shared circuit.

**Avoid:** dedicated POS and IT circuits, isolated from kitchen distribution panel where possible.

## FAQs

**How long does a restaurant electrical build-out take?**
12–20 weeks typical, lease to opening. Faster is possible with prefabbed kitchen equipment and tight sub coordination; slower is common when service drops are needed.

**What service size does a restaurant need?**
Fast-casual: 200–300 amps single or 3-phase. Full-service: 400+ amps 3-phase. Specialty (pizzerias, breweries with heavy kitchens): often 600+ amps.

**Does the landlord pay for the build-out?**
Varies by lease. Many TI (tenant improvement) packages cover base electrical to the panel; tenant pays kitchen-specific and finish-out. Read the lease and have an electrician review the existing service before signing.

**Can I use the prior tenant's equipment connections?**
Sometimes — if the prior tenant was a restaurant and the equipment is in the same locations. More often, layout changes require new circuits and disconnects.

**What's the most common inspection failure?**
Disconnect placement (not within sight of equipment) and hood interlock sequencing. Both are caught and corrected during rough inspection if drawings are accurate.
