A 100 amp panel was the residential standard for decades — perfectly adequate for a 1970s home with a gas range, gas dryer, window AC, and a TV. A modern home with central AC, electric range, electric dryer, EV charger, and a heat pump easily doubles that load. The result is that 100 amp panels are increasingly outgrown rather than failed, and 200 amp has become the new residential default. Here is how to tell which one your home needs.

## Quick Answer

- **100 amp:** OK for small homes (≤1,500 sq ft), gas-heavy appliances, no EV.
- **200 amp:** modern default; supports central AC, electric range, EV charger, future loads.
- **400 amp:** large homes, dual EV setups, workshops, heat pumps + electric range + EV combined.
- The deciding factor is **calculated load**, not square footage.
- A licensed electrician runs a load calculation per NEC Article 220.

## What "Amp Rating" Actually Means

The amp rating on your panel is the maximum current it can pass through the main breaker without tripping. A 100 amp panel passes 100 amps at 240V — equivalent to 24,000 watts of continuous total load. A 200 amp panel passes 200 amps = 48,000 watts.

This doesn't mean a 100 amp home is at risk if it has more than 24,000 watts of installed appliances. The NEC's load calculation method (Article 220) applies demand factors that account for the fact that not everything runs simultaneously. A typical 2,000 sq ft Michigan home with central AC and electric range comes out to a calculated load between 80 and 130 amps — fine on 100 amp service if no EV or heat pump, tight on 100 amp with either, comfortable on 200 amp with both.

## Signs Your Panel Is Undersized

Common indicators across the homes we service in Holland, Hudsonville, and Grand Rapids:

1. **Lights dim noticeably when the AC starts.** Brief dim is normal — sustained or dramatic dim means voltage sag from undersized service.
2. **Breakers trip when running multiple major appliances.** Range + microwave + dishwasher = trip.
3. **No spare breaker slots.** You can't add a circuit without removing one.
4. **Tandem (double-wide) breakers crammed into single slots.** Original installer ran out of room.
5. **Aluminum service entrance with corroded lugs.** Beyond just amperage, the service itself needs upgrading.
6. **Federal Pacific Stab-Lok or Zinsco panel.** Replace regardless of size — see <a href="/blog/zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement" data-ga-event="spoke_to_spoke_click" data-ga-label="100-amp-vs-200-amp-panel__zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement" data-ga-destination="/blog/zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement">our Zinsco/FPE guide</a>.

## When 100 Amp Is Still Enough

Don't assume an upgrade is needed if:

- Your home is under 1,500 sq ft with **gas heat, gas water heater, gas range, gas dryer**.
- You have window AC, not central.
- You have no EV and no plans for one.
- The panel has spare breaker slots and modern AFCI/GFCI protection.
- A load calculation shows your peak demand is well below 80 amps.

Plenty of small Holland and Saugatuck cottages run perfectly on 100 amp service indefinitely. Upgrading purely "for resale" without a load justification is rarely worth the spend.

## When 200 Amp Is the Right Call

- **Central AC** on top of normal household load.
- **Electric range** or electric water heater.
- **EV charger** (current or planned within 3 years).
- **Heat pump** retrofit or addition.
- **Workshop** with welder, table saw, air compressor.
- **Adding a second-floor or basement build-out.**
- **Resale market expectations** for homes >2,000 sq ft.

200 amp is also the right call any time the panel itself needs replacing (age, brand, damage) — the cost difference between a 100 amp and 200 amp panel replacement is typically just $200–$500.

## When 400 Amp Makes Sense

Rare in standard West Michigan residential, but justified for:

- Homes over 4,500 sq ft.
- All-electric homes with heat pump + electric range + dual EV.
- Detached shops with significant motorized equipment.
- Some lakeshore properties with boat lifts, pool equipment, and irrigation pumps.

## What an Upgrade Actually Costs

A standard 100 → 200 amp upgrade in West Michigan runs $2,800–$5,500 installed, depending on:

- Service entrance condition (often needs replacement at the same time)
- Meter base condition
- Utility coordination (who reconnects)
- Grounding electrode requirements (rod, plate, or water service bond)
- Whether the panel relocates
- Permit and inspection fees

For homes also adding generator backup or EV charging, combining the upgrades into one permit and one mobilization saves $300–$800. ## How an Electrician Decides

A load calculation, not a guess. We pull the panel, list every connected appliance and dedicated circuit, calculate connected and demand loads per NEC 220, and compare to the current service rating. Where peak demand exceeds 80% of service capacity, an upgrade is justified. The whole evaluation takes 30–45 minutes on site.

## FAQs

**How can I tell if I have 100 or 200 amp service?**
Look at the main breaker at the top of your panel — the number stamped on it (100, 150, 200) is the service rating. If unsure, take a photo to an electrician.

**Can I upgrade just the panel and keep the same service wires?**
Sometimes. The service entrance wire, meter base, and grounding all need to match the new amperage. We check each component before quoting.

**Does a panel upgrade increase home value?**
Usually $1,500–$4,000 in resale value, depending on the original panel's age and brand. Federal Pacific or Zinsco panels are appraisal liabilities; upgrade typically recovers cost on sale.

**How long does the install take?**
Standard 100 → 200 amp upgrade: 6–10 hours of work, plus a power outage of typically 2–4 hours while the utility cuts and reconnects. Same-day completion in most cases.

**Will my power bill change?**
No. Service amperage doesn't affect how much you pay per kWh. You only pay for energy used, not capacity available.
