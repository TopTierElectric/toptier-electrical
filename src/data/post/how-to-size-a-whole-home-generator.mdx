Sizing a whole-home generator for a Michigan house comes down to one calculation: the peak running load you want to support when the grid goes down. Too small and the unit shuts off under load. Too large and you've paid for capacity you'll never use. Here is the exact sizing process we walk Holland and Grand Rapids homeowners through.

## Quick Answer

- Most 2,000 sq ft Michigan homes need a **14–22 kW** whole-home generator.
- A 3,000+ sq ft home with central AC and an electric range typically needs **22–26 kW**.
- The deciding factor is **simultaneous load**, not square footage.
- Always size for the largest motor start (usually the AC compressor).
- Have a licensed electrician run a load calculation before you buy.

## What "Whole-Home" Actually Means

A whole-home generator is a permanently-installed standby unit that powers your entire electrical panel through an automatic transfer switch. When utility power drops, it senses the outage, starts within 10–30 seconds, and carries your full load until the grid returns. This is different from a portable generator, which only powers a few circuits through an interlock or extension cord.

The "size" of a generator is rated in kilowatts (kW). One kW = 1,000 watts of running power. Generators also have a higher _starting_ wattage rating to handle the inrush current when a motor (AC compressor, well pump, refrigerator) kicks on.

## The Load Math (How an Electrician Sizes It)

We use the NEC standard method or the optional method (NEC Article 220) to calculate connected load. A simplified residential version:

1. **Lights and general outlets:** 3 watts × heated square footage.
2. **Small appliance circuits:** 1,500 watts × 2 (kitchen).
3. **Laundry:** 1,500 watts.
4. **Specific appliances:** add the nameplate rating of range, dryer, water heater, AC, well pump, etc.
5. **Apply demand factors:** the NEC allows a percentage reduction on the first 10,000 W of general load and 40% on the remainder.
6. **Add the largest motor at 125%** of its starting amps.

For a typical 2,000 sq ft Michigan home with central AC, electric range, electric dryer, and a well pump, the running load lands between **14 and 18 kW**. Sizing the generator at 20–22 kW gives a healthy margin for motor inrush and future loads.

## Common Mistakes That Cause Undersizing

- **Ignoring well pumps and sump pumps.** Pump motors draw 6–8× their running watts at startup. In West Michigan's clay-soil basements, sump pumps are non-negotiable backup loads.
- **Counting average AC draw instead of peak.** A 3-ton AC pulls 3,500 W running but spikes to 12,000 W on startup.
- **Forgetting an EV charger.** A Level 2 charger adds 7.2–11.5 kW to your panel load. If you plan to install one in the next few years, size for it now.
- **Choosing by square footage alone.** A 1,500 sq ft home with electric heat draws more than a 3,000 sq ft home with gas heat.

## When to Size Up (vs Buying Bigger Than Needed)

Size up if you have or plan to add:

- Central AC with a 4+ ton compressor.
- An EV charger (or two).
- A heat pump.
- A workshop with welder or air compressor.
- A second sump or septic pump.

Size as calculated if your load profile is stable and the panel isn't slated for upgrades. Oversizing by 50%+ wastes money on fuel and maintenance — these units run a self-test every week.

## Michigan-Specific Notes

West Michigan winters drive two sizing realities: ice-storm outages can last 24–72 hours (so fuel autonomy matters), and furnace blower motors plus sump pumps are mandatory backup loads. Consumers Energy and DTE territory homeowners both lose power most often in December–March windstorms.

If you're on a propane fuel source, factor tank size into runtime: a 22 kW generator at half-load burns roughly 2.4 gallons of propane per hour. A 500-gallon tank gives you about 4 days at typical demand.

## When to Call a Pro

A whole-home generator install is a permitted job in Michigan. It requires a licensed electrician for the transfer switch and panel work, and usually a licensed plumber or gas fitter for the fuel line. ## FAQs

**What size generator do I need for a 2,000 sq ft house?**
For most 2,000 sq ft Michigan homes with central AC and standard appliances, 18–22 kW is the right range. A load calculation on your panel is the only way to be sure.

**Can I run my whole house on a 10 kW generator?**
Usually not all at once. A 10 kW unit can power essentials — furnace, fridge, lights, sump — but will trip if you start the AC or run the electric range at the same time.

**How much does a 22 kW Generac install cost in Michigan?**
Installed pricing varies with fuel source, transfer switch type, and panel condition. We provide a clear estimate after a site visit — no surprise add-ons.

**Do I need a permit for a whole-home generator?**
Yes. Michigan requires electrical and (for gas/propane) mechanical permits, plus a final inspection. We pull and close out all permits on our installs.

**How often does a standby generator need maintenance?**
Annual service is the manufacturer minimum: oil, filter, spark plugs, and a load-bank test. Most units self-test weekly without intervention.
