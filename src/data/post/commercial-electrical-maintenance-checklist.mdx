An annual electrical maintenance routine is the difference between a planned 2-hour service call and an unplanned 2-day shutdown. The 2023 edition of NFPA 70B is the first to make electrical maintenance a mandatory standard rather than a recommendation — and insurance carriers, AHJs, and OSHA increasingly expect facilities to be able to document compliance. Here is the practical checklist we run for West Michigan commercial customers.

## Quick Answer

- **Quarterly:** visual inspections, GFCI tests, exit/emergency lighting tests.
- **Annual:** infrared (IR) scan of panels and switchgear, torque check, breaker exercise.
- **3–5 year:** insulation resistance testing, neutral and ground inspection.
- **5 year:** transformer servicing, generator load-bank test.
- Most failures are predictable — IR scans catch 70–80% of them months in advance.

## Why NFPA 70B Now Matters

NFPA 70B (Standard for Electrical Equipment Maintenance) went from "recommended practice" to "standard" in 2023. Compliance is referenced in:

- **OSHA citations** for electrical-related workplace incidents.
- **Insurance carrier requirements** in many commercial policies.
- **AHJ inspections** during permit pulls or after incidents.
- **NFPA 70E arc-flash compliance**, which depends on equipment being maintained per 70B.

Most facilities can meet the standard with quarterly visual inspections and an annual professional inspection. Documentation matters as much as the work itself.

## The Quarterly Checklist (Internal Staff)

These items can be done by trained facility staff, no electrician needed:

1. **Test GFCI receptacles** in kitchens, restrooms, and outdoor locations. Press TEST; confirm the reset releases power.
2. **Test exit signs and emergency egress lighting.** Hold the test button or kill power to the circuit; confirm 90-minute battery backup runs.
3. **Inspect panel covers for damage, missing screws, or signs of moisture.**
4. **Walk the exterior** for damaged service entrance, leaning poles, missing weather covers.
5. **Check all receptacles** in customer-facing areas for visible damage or warmth.
6. **Confirm all panel breakers are labeled** and labels match reality.
7. **Document each inspection** in a simple maintenance log.

A 30-minute quarterly walk catches the easy stuff before it becomes a service call.

## The Annual Checklist (Licensed Electrician)

These items require a licensed electrician with appropriate test equipment:

### 1. Infrared (IR) thermography of panels and switchgear

The single highest-value test. An IR camera identifies hot spots in panels, breakers, and connection points before they fail. A 20°F rise above ambient is a warning; 50°F+ is an emergency. Document all readings.

### 2. Torque-check connections

Lugs, breaker terminals, and bus connections loosen over time from thermal cycling. Annual torque-check (per manufacturer spec) catches loose connections that cause IR hot spots.

### 3. Exercise all breakers

Toggle each breaker off and back on. This breaks up oxidation on the contacts and exposes stuck breakers. Sounds trivial; finds problems regularly.

### 4. Verify grounding

Inspect grounding electrode connections at the panel and at each ground rod. Aluminum/copper transitions are the highest-failure-rate locations.

### 5. Inspect arc-fault and ground-fault protection

Test all GFCI and AFCI devices. Replace any that don't trip on TEST or that have failed (visible damage, no light).

### 6. Walk emergency systems

Egress lighting, exit signs, fire alarm panel indicators. Test where safe.

### 7. Generator service (if applicable)

Annual maintenance per manufacturer: oil, filter, plugs, load-bank test. Whole-building TVSS units should be checked annually. Indicator lights signal worn-out modules.

Written report with photos, IR images, torque readings, and recommendations. This is your NFPA 70B paper trail.

## 3–5 Year Tests

### Insulation resistance (megohmmeter) testing

Tests for degraded insulation that hasn't yet caused a fault. Most useful on feeders, motors, and switchgear that haven't been inspected in years.

### Neutral and ground continuity verification

Confirms grounding integrity hasn't degraded. Particularly important in facilities that have added equipment over time.

### Switchgear cleaning

For facilities with switchgear or older switchboards, periodic cleaning extends life and catches problems behind dead-fronts.

## 5-Year Items

### Transformer service

Liquid-filled transformers need oil testing for moisture, dielectric strength, and dissolved gases. Dry-type transformers benefit from cleaning and torque-check.

### Full coordination study refresh

If your facility has expanded or had major equipment changes, the original protective device coordination study may no longer be accurate. Arc-flash labels become inaccurate as a result.

### Battery-backed emergency systems

Battery banks for emergency lighting, fire alarm, and IT UPS systems should be tested and capacity-verified.

## Red Flags Worth Calling About Immediately

Even between scheduled visits, call same-day if you see:

- Warm or hot breaker faces or panel covers.
- Buzzing, humming, or sizzling from a panel or transformer.
- Discoloration around outlets or switches.
- Repeated tripping of the same breaker.
- Flickering lights across multiple zones.
- Burning smell — especially in mechanical rooms or panel rooms.

These are signs of active degradation. Continuing operation often makes the eventual fix more expensive.

## FAQs

**Is NFPA 70B legally required?**
It's a standard, not a federal law. But it's referenced by OSHA, NEC 70E, and most commercial insurance policies — meaning compliance is effectively required for most facilities to avoid liability.

**Can my maintenance staff do everything?**
Quarterly visual inspections, yes. Annual IR scans and torque-checks require licensed-electrician training and PPE. Splitting work this way is standard and cost-effective.

**Do I need to do this on a leased space?**
Tenant responsibilities vary by lease. Often the landlord handles the service entrance and the tenant handles everything downstream of the meter. Read your lease; both sides' insurance carriers may require documentation.

**What's the difference between IR scan and a normal inspection?**
A normal inspection is visual and mechanical. An IR scan is thermographic — it sees temperature differences invisible to the eye and catches connection problems weeks or months before failure.

**How long does an annual inspection take?**
2–6 hours for typical commercial facilities (5–25k sq ft). Industrial sites with multiple distribution panels and motor control centers take a full day.
