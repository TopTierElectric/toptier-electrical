Buying a house and the inspection report mentions electrical issues? I get it — I bought one too. My own house was built in the 1860s and has been through multiple remodels over the years. I've opened walls and found layered generations of wire stacked on top of each other — different decades of work, different materials, different splice methods, all sharing the same cavity. So when a buyer hands me their inspection report with "electrical concerns" highlighted in yellow, I know exactly what's running through their head: _is this going to be a money pit?_ The honest answer for most homes is no — but there are a handful of specific things that change the math. Here's how I sort them out, both as an electrician and as someone who's lived through it.

## Quick Answer

- **Dealbreakers (negotiate hard or walk):** active Zinsco / Federal Pacific panels, ungrounded service entrance, K&T in contact with blown-in insulation.
- **Negotiable (ask for credit or seller fix):** aluminum branch wiring, two-prong outlets throughout, panel near end of life.
- **Live-with-it:** a handful of ungrounded outlets, older Square D / GE panel in good shape, K&T in attic with no insulation contact.
- **Always ask your inspector:** panel brand, service amperage, GFCI/AFCI coverage, signs of past DIY work.
- **Get a pre-purchase electrical walkthrough** if the inspection report flagged anything specific.

## What I Look For First — Before the Panel

Most people open an inspection report and jump straight to the panel section. I do the opposite. The panel matters, but it's not the highest-fire-risk item in most older homes. The first three things I check:

1. **Service entrance condition.** Where the utility lines hit the house. If the weatherhead is rusted through, the mast is leaning, or the conduit is full of water, the panel inside doesn't matter — the whole service has to come out.
2. **Grounding.** Is there a ground rod? Is the cold-water bond still intact (or did somebody replace a section of supply line with PEX and break the bonding path)? Ungrounded houses are a much bigger problem than a single ungrounded outlet, and they're invisible until you measure.
3. **Evidence of past DIY work.** This is the single biggest predictor of hidden problems. The patterns I see over and over: unsheathed wire (Romex with the outer jacket missing or stripped where it shouldn't be), open junctions (splices hanging in basements or attics with no box, or boxes with missing covers), and missing neutrals (especially on switch loops and multi-wire branch circuits that someone "improved" without understanding what they were touching). Mismatched wire colors, breakers that don't match the panel brand, junction boxes hidden behind drywall, and scorched outlet faces fall in the same category. Every one of these tells me the inspection report is the tip of the iceberg.

If those three are clean, the rest of the house is usually manageable. If any of them are bad, the panel rarely tells the whole story.

## The Inspection Report Decoder

Here's how I read the common "electrical concerns" language home inspectors use:

| Inspector wrote                               | What it usually means                                       | How serious                                               |
| --------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Federal Pacific / Zinsco panel observed"     | Panel brand with documented failure-to-trip history         | 🔴 Replace before move-in if possible                     |
| "Knob and tube wiring present"                | Original early-1900s wiring still energized                 | 🟡 Depends on insulation contact and modification history |
| "Aluminum branch wiring observed"             | 1965-1975 era aluminum on 15/20 amp circuits                | 🟡 Mitigation needed — see below                          |
| "No GFCI protection in [kitchen/bath/garage]" | Missing modern code-required protection                     | 🟢 Easy fix, cheap                                        |
| "Two-prong outlets throughout"                | Ungrounded receptacles                                      | 🟢 Multiple paths to fix                                  |
| "Double-tapped breakers"                      | Two wires on one breaker terminal where not allowed         | 🟢 Quick correction                                       |
| "Reverse polarity at [outlet]"                | Hot and neutral swapped                                     | 🟢 5-minute fix                                           |
| "Open ground at [outlet]"                     | Three-prong outlet with no ground wire connected            | 🟢 Depends on whether ground exists in the box            |
| "Service entrance cable damaged"              | Cracked/exposed insulation on the cable from meter to panel | 🔴 Replace before closing if possible                     |
| "Panel near capacity"                         | All breaker slots filled or amperage at limit               | 🟡 Plan for upgrade within 1-3 years                      |

The big distinction: panel brand and service condition are **systemic** problems — you have to fix the whole assembly. Most of the green-flag items are **point** problems — fix the specific outlet or circuit and move on.

## My Personal Take on the Big Three Concerns

### Knob-and-Tube Wiring

K&T is the wiring style used roughly 1880-1940. What actually matters when you find it:

- **Is it covered by blown-in insulation?** This is the real fire risk — K&T is designed to dissipate heat into open air. Bury it under cellulose and the heat has nowhere to go. Insurance carriers know this and ask specifically.
- **Has it been modified?** Original K&T installed in 1910 by a real electrician is more reliable than K&T spliced into a 1970s Romex addition with wire nuts in a wall cavity. The DIY mid-life modifications are where the danger lives.
- **Are the splice points accessible?** Open junction boxes you can inspect = fine. Buried splices behind drywall = you don't know what's in there.

De-energized K&T is a non-issue. Active K&T contained in known areas with no insulation contact is manageable. K&T buried in insulation or modified by previous owners is the version I'd negotiate hard on. See our [full K&T guide](/blog/knob-and-tube-wiring-what-to-do) for the mitigation paths.

### Aluminum Branch Wiring

Houses built between roughly 1965 and 1975 are the suspect window — that's when aluminum was used on 15 and 20 amp circuits before the failure modes were widely understood. The wiring itself isn't the problem. The connections at outlets, switches, and the panel are. Heat cycles loosen the connections over time, and loose connections at high current create arcing.

Three mitigation paths, cheapest to most thorough:

1. **CO/ALR rated devices** at every outlet and switch (devices specifically listed for aluminum).
2. **Listed wire connectors** at every termination — pigtail copper to the existing aluminum at each device, using connectors specifically listed for aluminum-to-copper splices.
3. **Full rewire** to copper.

Most insurers accept option 2. Option 1 is the bare minimum and not universally accepted anymore. Option 3 is overkill for most homes but the most permanent answer. Details in our [aluminum wiring guide](/blog/aluminum-wiring-what-to-do).

### Zinsco and Federal Pacific Panels

This is the one I tell buyers to actually push on. Both panel brands have documented failure-to-trip rates that mean breakers don't always open under overload — the exact moment they're supposed to protect the wiring. I don't care how clean the panel looks; if the cover says Federal Pacific, Stab-Lok, Zinsco, or Sylvania, plan on replacement. Most West Michigan home insurers either won't write a policy or surcharge the premium until it's replaced.

The good news: Zinsco/FPE replacement is a known-scope job. I do these regularly across West Michigan — not a surprise during closing. Details in our [Zinsco/FPE replacement guide](/blog/zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement).

## Service Amperage — Is 100 Amp Enough?

The other question I get from buyers of older homes: _can I live with 100 amp service?_ Honest answer: depends on **what's already in the house** plus what you plan to add. Walk through the home and inventory the major loads before you guess at the answer.

What to look at during your walkthrough:

- **Heating source** — gas furnace or boiler vs electric baseboard vs heat pump. Electric heat is the single biggest draw.
- **Water heater** — gas, electric tank, or tankless electric (tankless electric is a huge load).
- **Dryer** — gas or electric.
- **Range / oven** — gas or electric.
- **Central AC** — present? what tonnage?
- **Hot tub, pool pump, EV charger, workshop subpanel** — any of these existing?
- **What you plan to add** in years 1-3 — EV, heat pump conversion, kitchen remodel, etc.

Then match against this:

- Gas heat, gas water heater, gas dryer, no EV, no central AC: 100 amp is plenty.
- Electric range OR electric dryer OR central AC: still usually fine with 100 amp.
- Two of the three above + future EV charger: 200 amp is the smarter call.
- All-electric home (electric heat or heat pump + electric water heater + electric range + electric dryer): 200 amp minimum even before you add anything.
- All-electric + EV charger + hot tub or workshop: 200 amp sometimes isn't enough — 400 amp gets considered.

I wouldn't make a 200 amp upgrade a closing condition unless the panel itself is also failing or the house is already maxed out on a 100 amp service. It's better as a planned upgrade in year 2 or 3 of ownership, when you know what loads you actually use. Our [100 vs 200 amp guide](/blog/100-amp-vs-200-amp-panel) walks through the calculation.

## Negotiating Tactics That Actually Work

Most buyers either ask the seller to "fix the electrical" (too vague) or accept a flat credit (too small). The structure that works:

1. **Itemize.** Each line item separately — "replace Zinsco panel," "rewire aluminum branch circuits," "add GFCIs in kitchen/baths." Vague concessions get padded down.
2. **Get a real quote first.** A pre-purchase electrical walkthrough with a written scope and estimate gives you a number to negotiate against, not a guess.
3. **Pick the right ask for each item.** Dealbreakers → seller fixes before closing. Big-but-not-urgent → seller credit so you pick your own electrician. Small → credit or absorb.
4. **Don't accept "as-is" on electrical fire risks.** Cosmetic issues, deferred maintenance, sure. Federal Pacific or active K&T near insulation, no.

If you're under contract right now and need that walkthrough, that's exactly what I do as part of our [licensed electrical repair](/electrical-repairs) work — site visit, written scope, no obligation. A focused 30-minute look at the panel, service entrance, and the specific items in the inspector's report is usually enough to give you a real number for the negotiation table.

## The Insurance Angle

Worth mentioning because it can change the math: in West Michigan, the carriers I see most often (Auto-Owners, Frankenmuth, Hastings, State Farm, Farm Bureau) all have specific rules for older-home electrical. Common patterns:

- **Federal Pacific / Zinsco panel:** several carriers won't write a new policy until it's replaced.
- **Active knob-and-tube:** policy possible but often with a higher premium or an exclusion for electrical fire.
- **Aluminum branch without mitigation:** depends on the carrier; some require AlumiConn/COPALUM remediation; others write with a surcharge.
- **Two-prong outlets, missing GFCIs:** generally a non-issue for insurance.

Ask your insurance agent before closing what they'll accept. The answer drives which items become must-fix before move-in.

## What a Pre-Purchase Walkthrough Looks Like

For buyers I see often enough that I should describe what to expect:

1. **Review of inspection report** — I read it before I arrive so I know what's already been flagged.
2. **Service entrance + meter base check.** 5 minutes outside.
3. **Panel inspection with cover off.** Bus condition, breaker brand check, double-taps, neutral/ground separation, evidence of past arcing.
4. **Spot-check of receptacles** in kitchen, bathrooms, garage, basement, and a few bedrooms. Outlet tester for polarity, ground, and GFCI function.
5. **Visible K&T or aluminum trace** in the basement or attic if accessible.
6. **Written scope + estimate** for the items that need work, in priority order.

Walkthrough time on site: about an hour for a single-structure home, longer if there's a detached garage, outbuilding, or shop on the property that needs its own look. The written estimate covers each item separately so you can negotiate them line-by-line, not as a lump.

## FAQs

**Is it worth getting a separate electrical inspection before closing?**
If the home inspector flagged anything specific, yes — a focused 30-60 minute electrician walkthrough costs far less than negotiating blind. If the inspection report came back fully clean and the house is post-1990 with a modern panel, probably not.

**Can I close on a house with active knob-and-tube wiring?**
Yes, but check with your insurance carrier first. Some won't issue a new policy until the K&T is de-energized or removed in finished living spaces. Others write the policy with a higher premium or fire exclusion.

**Will my insurance company really refuse coverage for a Zinsco or Federal Pacific panel?**
Several West Michigan carriers do. Even those that don't refuse outright often surcharge the premium or require replacement within 90 days. Replace the panel first, then shop the policy — you'll get better rates.

**How much does a panel replacement add to a home purchase budget?**
It varies by site conditions, existing service entrance, grounding, amperage target, and whether the upgrade gets bundled with other work. We do a no-obligation pre-purchase walkthrough and provide a written estimate before you go back to the negotiating table.

**Can I do any of this electrical work myself after I buy the house?**
Some things, yes — swapping out an outlet, adding a GFCI, replacing a light fixture. Anything that touches the panel, the service entrance, or new circuits needs permits and a licensed electrician in Michigan. The work also needs to pass inspection for insurance and resale.

**What if the seller refuses to fix the major items?**
That's a signal worth taking seriously. If a seller won't address a Federal Pacific panel or service entrance issue with either a fix or a credit, the question becomes whether you can absorb that cost on day one of ownership. Sometimes walking is the right call.
