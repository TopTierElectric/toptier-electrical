A panel upgrade is one of the more disruptive electrical projects you can do in a home—and one of the more valuable. The right time to do it isn't "when the panel breaks." It's when the existing panel is full, outdated, damaged, unreliable, or no longer matches how the home actually uses electricity.

## Quick Answer

Consider a panel upgrade when one of these is true:

- The panel is full and you're adding load (EV, generator, HVAC, addition)
- The panel shows wear, corrosion, heat damage, or has a known problem brand
- Breakers trip during normal use, lights flicker under load, or circuits feel unreliable
- You're planning a remodel that adds significant electrical scope
- The service is undersized for what the home runs day-to-day
- The labeling is wrong, missing, or unsafe

If none of those apply, you probably don't need an upgrade right now. We'll tell you that.

## A Panel Upgrade Is About More Than Breaker Space

Empty slots are only one factor. Three things matter together:

1. **Service capacity** (the amperage the utility delivers and your panel can handle—100A, 150A, 200A, 400A)
2. **Breaker space** (physical slots for new circuits)
3. **Panel condition** (age, brand, wear, grounding, code compliance)

A panel can be at any combination of these and still need upgrading. A 200-amp panel with empty slots is in great shape only if the panel itself is healthy.

## Common Signs a Panel Upgrade May Be Needed

You don't need all of these—any one is worth a conversation:

- **Tripping breakers under normal use.** A breaker doing its job once is fine. The same breaker tripping every week is telling you something.
- **Warm or discolored breakers, scorch marks, or melted insulation.** Stop using the circuit and call.
- **Lights dim or flicker** when the AC, microwave, or dryer kicks on.
- **You're out of breaker spaces** and need to add a circuit.
- **Double-tapped breakers** (two wires under one terminal where it's not allowed).
- **Aluminum branch wiring concerns** in older homes (not the same as aluminum service entrance).
- **Known problem panel brands** (your electrician will recognize them).
- **Outdated fuse panels** that can't accept modern AFCI/GFCI breakers.
- **Corrosion, rust, or moisture** inside the panel.
- **Labels are wrong, missing, or unreadable.** Dangerous during emergencies.

## Future Loads That Can Change the Decision

Sometimes the panel is fine for what you have today but undersized for what's next. Common load additions that push a panel toward an upgrade:

- EV charging (especially 48A+ or two vehicles)
- Whole-home or large standby generator
- Heat pump or electric resistance heat
- Central AC upgrade
- Hot tub or pool equipment
- Addition, accessory dwelling unit, or finished basement
- Workshop with welders, dust collection, or large compressors
- Kitchen remodel with induction range and double oven

If two or more of these are on your near-term list, plan the panel work around the largest load—not the smallest.

## Panel Replacement vs Service Upgrade

These are not the same thing:

- **Panel replacement** swaps the panel and breakers but keeps the existing service size (the wire from the meter to the panel).
- **Service upgrade** changes the service size—usually 100A → 200A, or 200A → 400A. It involves the meter base, service entrance conductors, sometimes the mast, and utility coordination.

A panel replacement gets you a modern panel, fresh breakers, and clean labeling. A service upgrade gets you actual capacity. We recommend whichever solves the real problem, not the more expensive one.

## Why Labeling and Organization Matter

A panel that's clearly labeled saves time during emergencies, simplifies future service, and makes selling the home easier. We label every circuit, organize the layout, and walk you through it before closeout. It's not glamorous. It matters.

## Cost and Scope Factors

Panel upgrade pricing depends on:

- Service size (200A vs 400A, single vs split bus)
- Panel brand and feature set (AFCI/GFCI breaker count)
- Mast, meter base, and grounding work
- Drywall and finish work if the panel is moving
- Permit, inspection, and utility coordination
- Planned outage logistics

Most residential panel replacements are a single-day install with a planned outage window. Service upgrades and meter base work can stretch longer depending on utility timing.

## FAQs

**Will my power be off?**
Yes—most panel upgrades involve a planned outage window while the work is completed.

**How long does it take?**
Many residential panel replacements finish in a day. Service upgrades that involve the utility add scheduling time.

**Do I need a permit?**
Yes. We coordinate the permit and inspection.

**Can I keep the same panel location?**
Usually yes. We confirm clearances and code requirements before quoting.
