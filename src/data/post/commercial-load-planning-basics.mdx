Commercial load planning looks simple from the outside — add up the wattages and pick a service. The reality is that NEC Article 220 has a dozen rules about demand factors, continuous vs non-continuous loads, motor starting current, and how to size feeders versus service. Get any of them wrong and you either pay for capacity you don't need or undersize a service that trips under realistic load. Here is what we actually calculate when we plan a commercial service.

## Quick Answer

- **Connected load** = sum of every nameplate rating on the panel.
- **Demand load** = connected load with NEC Article 220 demand factors applied.
- **Continuous loads** (3+ hours) calculate at **125%** of nameplate.
- **The largest motor** gets a **25% adder** for inrush.
- **Service is sized for demand load**, not connected load.

## What "Load" Actually Means

Three distinct numbers, often used interchangeably and incorrectly:

1. **Connected load:** Total of every appliance and circuit nameplate, regardless of whether they ever run simultaneously.
2. **Demand load:** Connected load reduced by NEC demand factors that account for diversity (not everything runs at once).
3. **Peak demand:** Actual measured maximum load over a billing period.

A 200-amp service may have 320 amps of connected load and still be properly sized — because demand factors reduce the calculated demand to under 200 amps. Building owners often see the "320 amp" number and panic. The calculation is what matters.

## NEC Article 220: The Calculation Framework

Article 220 of the NEC defines exactly how to calculate load for service sizing. The high-level structure:

### Branch circuit loading
- General lighting and receptacles: by square footage and use type (Table 220.12).
- Specific loads (appliances, motors, signs): nameplate rating with code multipliers.

### Feeder and service loading
- Sum branch circuit loads.
- Apply demand factors per occupancy type (Tables 220.42, 220.44, 220.56, 220.84 for residential).
- Add largest motor at 125% of nameplate (430.24).
- Apply continuous load multiplier (125%) for loads that run 3+ hours continuously.

### Total service size
Larger of:
- Calculated demand load
- Largest single appliance + 25%
- A minimum based on occupancy type

The math is mechanical but the bookkeeping matters. A missed continuous-load multiplier on a 50-amp circuit makes the difference between an adequate and an inadequate service.

## Demand Factors That Matter Most

### Lighting demand
General lighting: 100% of first 12,500 VA, then **80%** of remainder for non-dwelling spaces (Table 220.42).

In a 25,000 sq ft retail space with 30 VA/sq ft lighting (750,000 VA total — extreme example), demand drops to roughly 80% of that. Smaller spaces don't benefit much from this factor.

### Receptacle demand
Non-dwelling general receptacle load: 100% of first 10 kVA, **50%** of remainder (Table 220.44).

This is significant in offices and retail where receptacle counts are high but coincident use is low.

### Kitchen equipment demand
Commercial kitchen demand factors (Table 220.56) heavily reduce calculated load for facilities with 3+ pieces of equipment: 90% for 3 units, 80% for 4, 65% for 5, 55% for 6+.

This factor alone can drop a 250-amp connected kitchen to a 150-amp demand load. It's why restaurant load calculations look so different from "add it all up."

### Motor demand
Largest motor gets a 25% adder; all other motors at nameplate. The 25% accounts for starting inrush current (typically 4–6× running current for 3–10 seconds).

## Continuous vs Non-Continuous Loads

Per NEC 215.3 and 230.42, **continuous loads** (running 3 hours or more) must be calculated at **125%** of their full-load current for conductor and overcurrent device sizing.

Examples of continuous loads:
- Lighting (any commercial space lit 3+ hours/day)
- Refrigeration motors
- HVAC fans during occupied hours
- Some IT/server loads

Examples of non-continuous loads:
- Coffee makers
- Mixers
- Most receptacle loads
- Vehicle lifts

This 125% multiplier is why a "20 amp" lighting circuit can only carry 16 amps of continuous load. Mis-applying this turns into trip-causing under-loading at design intent.

## Motor Loads: The Easy-to-Miss Items

Motors are the biggest source of underestimated load:

- **Locked rotor current** (the inrush at startup) is 4–6× running current.
- The **largest motor** in the system gets a 25% adder to feeder/service sizing.
- **Motors above 1 HP** need their own disconnect within sight.
- **VFDs** introduce different load characteristics — harmonic distortion can require oversized neutrals on 3-phase wye systems.

In commercial kitchens, the largest motor is often the walk-in compressor or the dishwasher booster pump. In retail, it's the HVAC compressor. In light industrial, it's whatever production equipment is biggest.

## Why Two Electricians Might Quote Different Service Sizes

Two licensed electricians can review the same space and quote 200-amp and 400-amp services. Common reasons:

1. **Different load assumptions for unspecified equipment.** "We're getting a 6-burner range" vs "we're getting a 10-burner griddle" changes the math.
2. **Different demand factor application.** Some electricians apply only the explicit factors; others use Article 220 optional methods.
3. **Future expansion margin.** One quote assumes today's load; the other adds 25–40% for foreseeable expansion.
4. **Different interpretation of continuous load durations.** What's "3 hours continuous" in your operation?
5. **Voltage drop considerations** for long feeder runs.

A good load calculation is documented, line-by-line, and matches the equipment list you provide. ## Planning for Future Loads

A 200-amp service that's calculated at 180-amp demand has no room for additions. Common future loads that catch facilities off-guard:

- **EV charging** for fleet or employees (often 40–80 amps per charger).
- **Heat pump conversions** for HVAC.
- **Server rooms or POS expansion.**
- **Additional production equipment.**

Sizing service with 20–30% headroom is the conventional approach for commercial work. ## What an Electrician Hands Off to the AHJ

A complete commercial load calculation package includes:

1. **Single-line diagram** of service and feeders.
2. **Panel schedule** with circuit assignments.
3. **Load calculation** showing connected load and demand calculation method.
4. **Voltage drop calculations** for long feeders.
5. **Coordination study** for selective tripping (if required by AHJ).
6. **Arc-flash analysis** with labels (NEC 130.5, where required).

## FAQs

**Why can't I just add up watts and divide by 240?**
You can — but the result is connected load, not demand load. Service sizing is based on demand load with NEC factors applied. The difference is often 30–50%.

**What's the most-overlooked load in commercial planning?**
EV charging for employees and customers. A single Level 2 charger adds 40–48 amps. A 6-charger employee setup adds 200+ amps to service requirements.

**Does the demand factor depend on building type?**
Yes — Article 220 has different demand tables for dwellings, schools, hospitals, and commercial occupancies. Mixed-use buildings calculate each portion separately.

**Should I plan for solar or storage in the load calc?**
Yes if you have any plan to add either within 5 years. Solar reduces effective load on certain circuits; battery storage may require dedicated transfer switching.

**How accurate is a load calculation vs actual measured peak demand?**
Properly done, calculated demand is usually within 10–20% of measured peak. Calculations are intentionally conservative to handle worst-case coincident loading.
