Flickering lights in a house can be anything from a loose bulb you can fix in 30 seconds to a failing main neutral that's one storm away from frying every appliance you own. The hard part is telling which is which. This guide walks through the seven most common causes in West Michigan homes and which ones can wait versus which need a same-day call.

## Quick Answer

- **One bulb flickers:** loose bulb or dying LED — replace it.
- **One room flickers:** loose connection in that circuit — call this week.
- **Whole-house flickers when AC or dryer starts:** normal voltage sag, usually OK.
- **Whole-house flickers randomly:** loose main connection or failing neutral — **call today**.
- **Lights brighten and dim across the house:** possible failing utility neutral — **call today, do not delay**.

## The Seven Most Common Causes

### 1. Loose or dying bulb (most common, least urgent)
A bulb backing out of its socket arcs slightly and flickers. LEDs nearing end of life flicker as their drivers fail. Replace the bulb and watch for 24 hours. If the new bulb also flickers, the problem is upstream.

### 2. Dimmer incompatibility
Many cheap LED bulbs aren't dimmable, and many dimmers are designed for incandescent loads. A flickering dimmable LED on a non-LED dimmer is a known-good-equipment mismatch, not an electrical fault. Replace the dimmer with an LED-rated one.

### 3. Loose connection in a switch, outlet, or fixture
Backstabbed wiring (pushed into spring clips on cheap devices) loosens over time. Lights on the same circuit will flicker, especially when a nearby switch is bumped or a vibration runs through the wall. This is a same-week call — a loose connection that arcs can scorch wood framing.

### 4. Voltage sag when a big motor starts
Central AC compressors, well pumps, and electric dryers pull 4–8× their running current at startup. Lights dimming briefly when the AC kicks on is normal, especially in older 100-amp service homes. If the dim is severe (noticeably darker, longer than half a second) or persistent, a panel upgrade may be warranted — see <a href="/blog/100-amp-vs-200-amp-panel" data-ga-event="spoke_to_spoke_click" data-ga-label="flickering-lights-when-to-worry__100-amp-vs-200-amp-panel" data-ga-destination="/blog/100-amp-vs-200-amp-panel">our panel size guide</a>.

### 5. Overloaded circuit
A circuit running near its capacity will produce small voltage drops as loads cycle on and off. The fix is rebalancing the loads or adding a circuit — not heavier-gauge wire on the same breaker (which doesn't help and may violate code).

### 6. Failing main breaker or panel busbar
An aging main breaker can develop intermittent contact issues that show up as whole-house flickering. Panels with known busbar problems — particularly Federal Pacific Stab-Lok and Zinsco — should be replaced regardless. See <a href="/blog/zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement" data-ga-event="spoke_to_spoke_click" data-ga-label="flickering-lights-when-to-worry__zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement" data-ga-destination="/blog/zinsco-fpe-panel-replacement">our guide on Zinsco and FPE panels</a>.

### 7. Failing utility neutral (the emergency case)
The most dangerous cause. The neutral conductor between your meter and the transformer carries the imbalanced current between your two 120V legs. When it fails or develops a high-resistance connection, the voltage on each leg becomes unstable: one leg drifts up to 140V+ while the other drops, depending on which side has more load. Symptoms:

- Lights on one side of the house brighten while lights on the other dim.
- LED bulbs and electronics fail in clusters.
- Appliances make humming or buzzing noises.
- Microwaves or refrigerators reset themselves.

This is a utility-side problem most of the time. **Call the utility's emergency line immediately** (Consumers Energy, DTE, or Holland BPW depending on your service). An electrician can confirm by measuring leg-to-neutral voltages on the panel.

## How to Triage in 60 Seconds

| Pattern | Likely cause | Call timing |
|---|---|---|
| One bulb | Bulb / fixture | Replace, watch |
| One room, all bulbs | Loose connection on circuit | This week |
| Whole house when AC starts | Voltage sag | Optional — usually OK |
| Whole house, random | Main breaker / panel | Same day |
| Brightening + dimming on opposite sides | Failing neutral | **Call utility now** |
| Combined with burning smell | Active fault | **Turn off main, call now** |

## What an Electrician Checks

A diagnostic visit for flickering typically runs through:

1. Voltage measurements at the panel on both legs.
2. Voltage stability under load (turning major appliances on/off).
3. Visual panel inspection for hot connections, discoloration, or aluminum wire issues.
4. Receptacle tester run on representative outlets on the affected circuit.
5. Targeted IR thermometer check on suspect connections.

In our experience across Holland, Hudsonville, and Grand Rapids service calls, about half of "flickering" calls turn out to be circuit-level loose connections that take an hour to fix. About a quarter are loads-too-big-for-panel situations. The rest are mixed — failing breakers, utility-side neutrals, and the occasional rodent-damaged wire in an attic.

## When to Call a Pro

Same-day if the flickering is whole-house and random, if it correlates with a burning smell, if it's worsening, or if bulbs and electronics are failing more often than usual. ## FAQs

**Why do my lights flicker when my AC turns on?**
Briefly, when the compressor starts and pulls 4–6× its running current. This is normal in 100-amp service homes. If the dim is severe or lasts more than half a second, the panel may be undersized.

**Can flickering lights cause a fire?**
The flickering itself doesn't cause fires — but the loose connection or arcing fault behind some types of flickering can. Persistent unexplained flickering should be diagnosed.

**Why do my lights flicker only at night?**
Often because LED bulbs are more visibly flickery in dark rooms. The flicker is happening during the day too; you just don't see it. Replace the bulb or check the dimmer compatibility.

**Should I replace bulbs before calling an electrician?**
Yes — bulb replacement is the cheapest, fastest first step. If new bulbs flicker on the same circuit, you've eliminated the bulb and confirmed the fault is electrical.

**My power company says it's not them. What now?**
A licensed electrician can confirm leg-to-neutral voltages and identify whether the issue is utility-side or inside your service. If we measure unbalanced voltages at the meter, we'll back up your utility callback.
