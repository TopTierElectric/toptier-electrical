A breaker that keeps tripping isn't broken. It's working. It's detecting a condition it's designed to interrupt—and it's protecting your home from an overheated wire, a short circuit, or a ground fault. The job is to figure out *why*, not to keep resetting it.

## Quick Answer

Common causes of a tripping breaker:

- **Overload**—too much current on the circuit (too many things on at once)
- **Short circuit**—a hot wire touches a neutral or ground
- **Ground fault**—current is leaking to ground (wet conditions, damaged insulation)
- **Failing equipment**—a motor, appliance, or fixture is drawing more than it should
- **Damaged wiring**—nicked insulation, rodent damage, pinched cable
- **Loose connection**—at the breaker, receptacle, or junction box
- **Failing breaker itself**—rare but possible, especially on older or known-problem panels

If a breaker trips repeatedly, stop resetting it and call an electrician.

## What a Breaker Is Trying to Protect

Three jobs in one device:

1. **Overcurrent**—prevents the wire from overheating when load exceeds rating
2. **Short-circuit**—interrupts a sudden, massive spike in current
3. **Ground fault** (GFCI breakers) or **arc fault** (AFCI breakers)—detects specific dangerous conditions

A breaker tripping means one of those events happened. Resetting it without finding the cause assumes the event won't repeat. That's a bad bet.

## Common Reasons Breakers Trip

### Overload (Most Common)

A 15-amp circuit can run about 1,800 watts continuously. Plug a 1,500-watt space heater into a circuit that's already running a TV, lamps, and a phone charger and you're at the edge. Over time, the breaker trips. The fix is usually to move loads to another circuit or add a dedicated circuit for the high-draw item.

### Short Circuit

A hot wire contacts a neutral or ground directly. The current spike is huge and fast. The breaker trips immediately. Usually the cause is damaged insulation, a faulty appliance, or something pinched during construction. Smell of burnt plastic is a strong indicator.

### Ground Fault

Current escaping the intended path—usually to ground through water, damaged insulation, or a faulty appliance. GFCI breakers and outlets catch this and trip. Common in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, and outdoor circuits where moisture is a factor.

### Arc Fault

AFCI breakers detect the unique signature of an arcing connection—loose wiring, damaged cable, or a failing splice. Arcing is what starts most electrical fires. AFCI tripping is sometimes nuisance (motor brushes, vacuum cleaners), but persistent tripping points to a real wiring issue worth investigating.

### Failing Equipment

A motor with worn bearings draws more current. A failing compressor pulls heavy startup load. A space heater with a damaged element shorts intermittently. The breaker isn't the problem; the appliance is.

### Loose Connections

Wires under terminals can loosen over time, especially on aluminum branch circuits or under heavy load. Loose connections create heat, arcing, and intermittent trips. They're hidden until someone pulls the panel cover or the receptacle.

### Failing Breaker

Rare. But breakers do age, and certain brands have known reliability issues. If everything else checks out and the same breaker trips on different loads, the breaker itself may be replaced.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't keep resetting** a breaker that trips repeatedly. Each trip can mean heat is building somewhere you can't see.
- **Don't replace a breaker with a higher amperage** to "stop the tripping." That bypasses the protection the breaker provides and can overheat the wire.
- **Don't wrap the trip lever** or otherwise defeat the breaker.
- **Don't ignore burning smells**, warm receptacles, or scorch marks. Those are urgent.

## What an Electrician Checks

A systematic diagnostic looks at:

- Which breaker, how often, under what conditions
- Whether the trip is immediate or delayed
- Total connected load on the circuit
- Receptacles, switches, and junction boxes for heat or damage
- Wire condition where accessible
- The breaker itself (and the panel for related issues)
- Any recent changes—new appliance, remodel, rodent activity, water intrusion

The goal is to find the actual cause, not to make the symptom go away.

## When It Is Urgent

Stop and call right away if you see:

- Burning smell from outlets, switches, or the panel
- Warm or discolored receptacles
- Visible scorch marks
- Sparking when plugging in
- Smoke or melted plastic
- Repeated trips of the main breaker

These are not "I'll get to it next week" problems.

## Cost and Scope Factors

Troubleshooting a tripping breaker prices out based on:

- How systematic the diagnosis needs to be (intermittent issues take longer)
- Whether wiring needs to be opened up to inspect
- Repair scope once the cause is found (replace receptacle, run new wire, fix splice)
- Whether breaker or panel work is needed

A simple "appliance is bad" diagnosis is fast. A hidden splice burning behind drywall takes longer. We confirm scope before starting work either way.

## FAQs

**Is it safe to reset a breaker once?**
Once is generally fine. Repeatedly is not.

**Do I need to be home for diagnostic work?**
Usually yes—we may need to access multiple rooms and test loads.

**How long does troubleshooting take?**
Anywhere from an hour to several hours depending on the cause. Intermittent issues take longer.

**Can it be the breaker itself?**
Sometimes. We rule out load and wiring issues first.
